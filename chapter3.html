Chapter 3: "Eleven Minutes"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text The alarm does not stop for eleven minutes. Gepard knows, because he is counting. One hand braced against the concrete wall beside the outpost door, the other still warm —still burning from where her fingers had been laced into his hair— and he is counting every second of that alarm the way a drowning man counts the surface above him, because it might be the only fixed point he has left. Six hundred and sixty seconds of that blaring, zone-wide shriek, methodically dismantling the small world they had built inside this room, until nothing remains of it but the shallow indentations her boots left in the thin crescent of snow that had blown through the door when she arrived. Then silence. Then her voice. "Sector A is reporting a Fragmentum convergence at the North ridge." She is already reading from the tactical display at her wrist, standing at the far end of the room with her back to him, every cable re-secured and tucked neatly behind her pauldrons. Commander Bronya. He watches the shift happen in real time; the line of her shoulders setting themselves with intention, her spine making a decision, every soft and unguarded thing about the last thirty seconds rearranging itself into something armored and elsewhere. "Estimated density: high. They're requesting reinforcements." A pause. He hears her exhale — slow, deliberate, the particular sound of pressing something back to a manageable depth. "It's near the ridge where we were earlier." She looks at him over her shoulder, and her eyes are sharp. Present. Already somewhere he is not. "I need to return to the palace before the patrol units notice my absence." "I know." He says. Because it is the only thing he knows for certain right now, and he has always preferred certainty to the alternative. "I'll take the East corridor back." She pulls her hood up. Not the cloak — that she folds over the chair with a mechanical neatness that makes something in his chest pull sideways — but the isothermal hood, the one that covers everything but her eyes. She picks up her face mask from the desk and holds it for a moment, half-raised, not putting it on yet. "Send word to Sector A that reinforcements are incoming." Brisk. Professional. The Commander's voice, the one that had spent the last twenty minutes being notably absent. "Standard suppression formation. I want hourly status updates until the bloom is confirmed cold." "Understood." "And Gepard." He waits. Something moves through her expression — there and gone, so quickly he cannot name it. She lowers the mask. "Stay inside the perimeter." He nearly says something. He can feel the something sitting at the back of his throat, and he makes the correct and responsible decision to leave it there. "Of course." She puts the mask on. She goes. The door closes behind her with its usual soft thud, and the outpost goes out like a lamp. —— He runs. It is completely unprofessional and he does not slow down once. Three minutes to Sector A at a full sprint through the blizzard, and he does not mind the cold tonight — the cold is useful, actually, shockingly so, and he drags lungfuls of frozen air into his chest and lets it do its work, scouring his thoughts into something flatter and more manageable with every stride. He focuses on the ground under his boots. The sound of packed snow. The orange hum of his pauldrons, their charge still holding strong. The sound of Sector A growing louder around the bend of the North ridge. He hears it before he sees it. A large Fragmentum convergence has a sound unlike anything else in the Restricted Zone — a sound no amount of years on the front line ever fully normalizes. A low-frequency thrum, deep enough to settle in the sternum rather than the ears, layered over a wet cracking noise like ice fracturing from below, and underneath both of those, if you are close enough, and he always ends up close enough: something that is not quite breathing, but that your body insists on treating as such anyway. He clears the ridge. The bloom is roughly forty meters wide. A mass of amber and black erupting from the snowfield ahead, crystalline spires jutting upward at angles that suggest no awareness of how angles are supposed to work, the ground around their base heaving very slowly and very steadily, like a set of lungs. Eight soldiers already in perimeter formation, geomarrow shields up and lances deployed, their breath rising in short controlled bursts. Two more at the portable charge station on the ridge's lip, the meter spinning urgently. One soldier — Mira, he recognizes her by the dent in her left pauldron from a Fragmentum impact three weeks ago that he has been after her to get repaired — is managing to hold the southern flank alone despite the bloom pushing steadily in her direction, and he makes a note to tell her so later. "Commander on site!" "Report," He says, and draws his weapon without breaking stride. "Convergence started twenty minutes ago, sir. Growth rate is slow but the density keeps climbing — we've pushed it back twice and it just redirects." The soldier speaking is young; Gepard doesn't know his name yet, which means he's newer than two months, which means he has never seen a convergence this size before, which explains the very specific quality of controlled terror in his voice. "It's different from the ones last week, sir. The ones last week felt random. This one feels like it knows where we are." Gepard looks at the bloom for exactly three seconds. Behavioral. Pela's word from the report surfaces in his mind, and he files it and moves on. "Yuna — south flank with Mira, don't let it push toward the Energy Hub." The soldier is already moving. "Chen, you're with me on the front line. Everyone else — suppression formation, maintain the perimeter, nothing crosses the line." He locks eyes with the young soldier briefly. "What's your name?" "Rikov, sir." "Rikov. You're doing fine. Stay in formation." The boy straightens. And then they push. Fragmentum combat at this scale is nothing like the clean, decisive engagements in Belobog's training manuals. It is grinding, loud, relentless, deeply inelegant work — the kind that leaves bruises in places you don't notice until the next morning and a particular ringing in the ears that takes two days to fully clear. His geomarrow shield takes the first impact and he feels it in both elbows, driven three steps back, boots carving furrows in the packed snow. He drives forward again, leans his whole body into it, and fires a concentrated burst of ice-lance energy at the nearest spire formation. It shatters. Three more grow back from the fracture points within seconds. He fires again. He does not stop. This is the part they don't depict in the palace murals. The murals show the General standing at the head of a formation, shield raised, the Fragmentum retreating before him in dramatic amber-lit defeat. The murals do not show two hours of brutal, grinding attrition in the teeth of a blizzard, while the Fragmentum grows and redirects and grows again with a patience that genuinely feels like intent, and the only real strategy available is simply to outlast it. They outlast it. The bloom collapses at the third hour. Not dramatically — it doesn't shatter or retreat so much as it simply exhales. The amber light dims. The ground stops moving. The crystalline structures crack from the base and crumble inward, and the sound drops from that deep unsettling thrum to nothing, and the nothing is almost louder. Gepard stands in the center of the cleared snowfield in the silence that follows, his charge at twenty-three percent, one hand on his knee, and breathes. His soldiers are quiet around him in the particular way that follows something that required everything — not unhappy, not relieved exactly, just hollow in a way that only sleep can refill. "Good work." He says. And then, because the young soldier is still standing nearby with the expression of someone who narrowly escaped something and hasn't fully processed it yet: "All of you. Solid formation tonight." Rikov exhales like he'd been holding it for three hours, which he probably had been. Gepard straightens. He looks north, toward the ridge, toward the distant dark mass of Qlipoth palace with its upper spires just catching the first suggestion of coming dawn. She'll be back by now. Inside. Safe behind the walls. He tells himself this is all he needs to know, and he is mostly successful. "Sir." Chen appears at his shoulder, holding out a small sealed report cylinder. "The charge station team documented the bloom's growth patterns. Pela's people asked us to collect real-time data whenever we have a large convergence." "Good thinking." He takes it. "Get everyone back to the main post for debrief. I'll be there in ten minutes." Chen nods and moves off. Gepard stands alone in the scorched snowfield for a moment longer, and in the quiet, he notices something he didn't have time to notice during the fight. The residual Fragmentum crystallization on the ground — the fragments too small to pose a threat, the ambient debris left after a bloom collapses — is not dispersed evenly across the site, the way it usually is. It is concentrated on the northern edge of the clearing. Pointing, very deliberately, toward Belobog's outer wall. He stares at this for a long moment. Then he takes out his phone and photographs it. —— He does not sleep before the morning meeting. This is not a decision so much as a thing that simply happens to him, in the way many things have been happening to him lately — he never finds the right moment, and then dawn arrives anyway. The war council chamber on the second floor of the palace's east wing is a room he knows with the familiarity of years. Long table, chairs designed by someone who had apparently never sat in one, maps of Belobog and the surrounding Restricted Zone spread across every surface, standing lamps casting pale yellow light because the north-facing windows offer the sun almost nothing to work with. He arrives first, which is habit and slightly excessive, and spends the waiting time reading a border assessment on his feet rather than sit down and have nothing to do with his hands. The others filter in. Administrator Pela arrives with the specific energy of someone who has been awake since four AM and has made peace with it entirely. She sets down a tower of folders, accepts a cup of coffee from a palace aide with the reverence typically reserved for religious ceremonies, and immediately begins cross-referencing three documents at once. She glances up at Gepard. "You were at the Sector A convergence last night." "Yes." "I heard the bloom pattern was atypical." "I sent you a photograph of the residual distribution at 4 AM." She checks her device, and her expression sharpens the way it does when data confirms something she suspected and wished it hadn't. "I'll have a full analysis ready by end of day." A pause, her finger tapping the edge of the table. "Gepard. How long has the bloom orientation been directional?" "That's the first time I've seen it this clearly. But looking back at the past month's patrol reports—" He opens his folder to a marked page and pushes it across the table. "The growth vectors have been incrementally northward across all three sectors for the last six weeks. I thought it was environmental variance." Pela stares at the page. She stares at it long enough that two other people enter the room and sit down before she speaks. "It's not environmental variance," she says, with the quiet, controlled devastation of someone delivering a diagnosis they had been hoping to avoid. "No," he agrees. "I don't think it is." Seele arrives like weather — fast, slightly disorganized in the way that is somehow always purposeful, her silver hair loose in a way that suggests she woke up twenty minutes ago and considered this a reasonable preparation time. She drops into the seat beside Pela, glances at the report, and looks up at Gepard. "Is this the bloom data from last night?" "Pela's running analysis—" "The Fragmentum is moving toward the populated sectors." Flat. Not a question. Something in her jaw sets. She spent her formative years in the Underworld — navigating a city the surface considered expendable — and Gepard has noticed that threats to civilians draw from Seele a specific quality of cold fury, very different from the hot kind, the kind that moves precisely and does not miss. "How long have we known." "We're establishing the timeline now," Pela says carefully. "How long have we suspected." A pause. "Possibly six weeks," Gepard says. Seele's eyes move to him, and he receives the full weight of that, and then she gives one sharp, accepting, no-time-to-waste nod, and opens her folder. Sampo Koski arrives last, because Sampo Koski always arrives last, and always arrives in a way that makes it feel like a choice rather than a failure of punctuality. He comes through the door without knocking — which, Gepard has gathered, is not rudeness so much as a deeply held personal philosophy about the nature of doors — spots the three of them already arranged in various stages of grim professional readiness, and pauses. "Hm," he says, reading the room with the practiced efficiency of a man whose survival has always depended on reading rooms quickly. "So it's that kind of morning." "Sit down, Sampo," Pela says. "Gladly." He takes the remaining chair, drops into it with the easy confidence of someone who belongs wherever he happens to be sitting, and sets a small folded piece of paper on the table in front of him. "I brought notes." Seele stares at the folded paper. "Are those written on the back of a playing card." "I work with available materials." He unfolds it. "Don't worry, it's all very professional. I used complete sentences and everything." Seele's expression does the thing it does in Sampo's presence, which is cycle visibly through several emotional weather systems before settling on a cold front. Gepard has always found Seele and Sampo's dynamic instructive — the specific texture of an old acquaintance relationship forged in the Underworld's particular school of hard lessons, which produces a familiarity that looks, to the uninformed observer, a lot like hostility. He suspects there is genuine warmth underneath it. He also suspects neither of them would appreciate him saying so. Bronya enters. The room does what it always does when she enters — a subtle collective reorganization, nothing dramatic, nothing anyone would admit to if asked. Just the slight shift that happens around a gravitational center. She is wearing the formal Guardian's coat over her armor today, the deep navy one with the gold shoulder trim, her silver hair pinned at the base of her neck. She looks exactly like what she is: twenty-two years old and carrying a country on the same shoulders that were pressed against a concrete wall three nights ago, whispering things she doesn't say in daylight. She sits at the head of the table, and she looks at Gepard first. The look lasts one second and communicates several things at once: you were at the convergence last night, you have not slept, and we are not going to discuss either of these things in this room. He responds with a look that says: understood. She looks briefly at Sampo in a way that communicates something else, something that has the shape of of course you're here, and Sampo responds to this with a small, precise nod that has none of his usual theater in it — the gesture of a man in a professional context he takes seriously despite every reasonable expectation to the contrary. "I'll keep this brief," she says, and opens her briefing folder. "The Sector A convergence last night has yielded data consistent with patterns we've been observing across all three sectors for the past several weeks. Pela, your analysis." Pela presents for eight minutes. She is extraordinary at this — at taking deeply alarming information and delivering it in a register calibrated to prevent panic without verging into dishonesty, threading that particular needle with the precision of long practice. Behavioral clustering. Directional growth. Consistent orientation toward population centers across all observed sites. The word intelligence continues to go unspoken. Everyone in the room hears it anyway. "There is one additional finding," Pela says, and her voice undergoes a very slight flattening — the specific register she uses when she is about to say something she would very much prefer not to. "The convergence last night reached a density level we haven't recorded since the event at Everwinter Hill." She pauses. "And the Everwinter Hill event was not a naturally occurring convergence." The room is quiet. "Something triggered it," Sampo says. He is not performing anything right now. His voice is even, his expression unreadable, the playing card notes folded neatly under two fingers on the table. This is, Gepard reflects, what Sampo Koski looks like when something has actually gotten his attention — stripped of every affectation, just a very sharp mind turning a problem over. "The energy signatures are similar. I am not drawing conclusions beyond that until the analysis is complete," Pela says. Bronya has not moved. She is looking at the map in the center of the table, her fingertip resting on the paper's edge with a faint, continuous pressure. "Thank you, Pela." Her voice is completely level. "Gepard — increase patrol frequency across all three sectors. Stagger the rotations so the schedule remains unpredictable." Her eyes move to him. "I want a second observation post established on the North ridge by end of week." "Already started the requisition." The almost-smile. There and gone. "Of course you have." She looks around the table. "Seele — coordinate with the Underworld district administrators. We'll increase civilian alert capacity without increasing visible military presence in the streets. I don't want people frightened before we understand what we're dealing with." "Understood," Seele says. No hesitation. "Sampo." The way Bronya says his name is different from how she says everyone else's — not unkind, not quite formal, the tone of someone who has decided to be pragmatic about the fact that a useful person is a useful person regardless of their relationship with convention. "The outer tunnel networks beneath the Restricted Zone's northern perimeter. You have people down there." It is not a question. Sampo looks at her for a moment. Something passes between them — a brief, mutual acknowledgment of a particular kind of transaction, the kind where both parties understand exactly what is being asked and exactly what it costs. "I might know some individuals with eyes in those corridors, yes." "If the Fragmentum is moving toward population centers through the surface, it may be using the tunnels as a secondary vector. I need to know if the crystallization has reached the tunnel walls. And I need to know without triggering a public report that goes through twelve administrative desks before it reaches mine." She holds his gaze. "Quietly." "Quietly," he repeats, with the air of a man who has built an entire career on that word. "I can do quietly." "Good." A pause. The faintest shift in her expression — not quite gratitude, not quite warning, something precisely between them. "Whatever it costs." "Consider it a community service," Sampo says, pleasantly, and picks up his folded notes. "I do those sometimes. It surprises people." "Good. Reports from all stations by 1800 hours. That's all." The room begins to move. Gepard stays seated a moment longer than the others, which he does not bother pretending is accidental, and when the chairs have cleared and the door has closed, it is the two of them and the north-facing windows and the pale morning light. "The signature Pela mentioned," he says. "Yes." "You already knew." She is quiet for a moment. "I suspected." "And the thing you weren't ready to say." She looks at him fully. Her eyes are very grey in the colorless northern light. "Not yet," she says. "But soon." He nods. He does not push. He never pushes — this has been the compact between them for years, unspoken and unbroken, the agreement that has survived more than most things in both their lives. She will tell him when she can. He will still be there when she does. "Gepard." He stops in the act of standing. "You look terrible." "Thank you, Commander." "Sleep before the evening patrol. That is not a suggestion." "Noted." "It's an order." He looks at her. She looks at him, and does not flinch, and does not soften, and does not look away. "...Understood," he says. —— He goes first to the Underworld's medical ward, because he promised Serval three days ago that he would check on a soldier recovering there and he is already twenty minutes late on that promise and feels appropriately guilty about it. The ward occupies a converted warehouse on the border of the second district. Natasha runs it the way she runs everything — with a quiet, total, slightly alarming competence, and an organizational logic that is not immediately legible but turns out, once you understand it, to be extremely efficient. The place is clean with the thoroughness of a personal philosophy. He has been here four times in two months and has yet to find a single thing out of place. Natasha is at the front desk when he enters, writing in a patient chart with the focused serenity of a person who has long made peace with the fact that there will always be more to do, and the only reasonable response to this is to keep doing it. She looks up. "Commander Gepard. You're late." "I know. I apologize." "Don't apologize to me." She closes the chart. "Private Shen has been awake since six AM and will absolutely not admit he's been listening for the door. He's doing well — Fragmentum crystallization has retreated to under four percent on the affected tissue. Another week and he'll be cleared for light duty." "That's good news." "It is." She comes around the desk and looks at him the way doctors look at people who present at a medical facility to visit a patient and are themselves in more obvious need of attention. "When did you last eat a full meal." "That's—" "I'm not accusing you of anything. The answer is on your face whether you give it to me verbally or not." She folds her hands — a gesture that reminds him, for a split second, of Bronya, though the warmth behind it is entirely different. Bronya's folded hands are a fortress. Natasha's are an open door. "The Sector A convergence was last night." "Yes." "You were there." "Yes." "And the Supreme Guardian has, I assume, already issued the same observation I'm currently making." He looks at her. "You speak with her often." "She sends reports. I send recommendations she mostly ignores." A brief, dry smile. "We have an arrangement." She turns and gestures for him to follow her. "Come. He's been asking about you." Private Shen is twenty years old and embarrassingly grateful to see him, in the way young soldiers get when a superior takes time to come in person, and Gepard spends twenty minutes at his bedside doing the part of the job that no mural ever depicts, which is simply being present. He tells him about the convergence last night, about Rikov holding the southern formation, about the bloom's collapse at the third hour. Shen laughs at the description of Rikov's face after it was over, and the laugh sounds like something releasing, like a held breath, and Gepard considers twenty minutes well spent. He is almost to the exit when the front door opens. Sampo Koski walks in carrying a paper bag that smells excellent. He is still wearing what he was wearing in the war council chamber three hours ago, which means he came here directly after the meeting, which means this is not the coincidence he is about to claim it is. He stops when he sees Gepard. His face does several interesting things in quick succession. "Commander." Not brightly this time — a genuine acknowledgment, the meeting still sitting somewhere behind his eyes. Then, recovering: "Another coincidence." "You followed me here from the palace." "I was heading this way regardless." He holds up the bag. "I bring food to the ward twice a week. Ask anyone." "Sampo." They both turn. Natasha is standing in the hallway behind them, chart in hand, with the expression of a person who understands something completely and has decided, as a matter of ongoing policy, not to make an issue of it. "Twice a week," she confirms, to Gepard, in a tone of absolute neutrality. Then, to Sampo: "If those are from the Tier Two bakery, tell me you got the ones with the dark filling." Sampo opens the bag, checks, looks up. "Dark filling." "Good." She extends her hand. He crosses the room and gives her the bag, and for a moment, as the handoff happens, neither of them moves particularly quickly about it. Natasha looks at the bag. Sampo looks at Natasha, and something in his face goes quiet in the specific way it had gone quiet in the council chamber when Pela said energy signature similar to Everwinter Hill — that stripped, unexpectedly sincere thing — except this version is warmer and considerably more difficult to look at directly. It slides back into place before it fully surfaces. "Break room," she says. "Of course," he says. She walks away down the hall. He watches her go with the practiced patience of a man who has been watching someone walk away for long enough that he's gotten very good at making it look casual. Then he seems to remember Gepard exists, and turns back. "So." He glances toward the hall once more, a flicker. "She works too much, you know." "I'm aware of the irony of you saying that to me." Sampo grins, and this one is entirely genuine. "That's what I like about you, Gepard. You're funny for someone who never laughs." "I laugh." "Name a time." He considers this with genuine effort. He cannot, at present, produce a specific example. "The meeting," Sampo says, and the grin fades to something more even. "The Stellaron data Pela held back. She held some back — you could tell from how she phrased the bit about the analysis not being complete. There's something she knows and isn't ready to say in a room with that many people in it." He is not asking. He is reporting, in his particular offhand way, that he noticed. "The Supreme Guardian knows what it is already." Gepard looks at him. Sampo shrugs lightly, folding his hands behind his head. "Just an observation. I notice things. It's a habit, mostly keeps me alive." He pauses. "I'll get the tunnel reports to you by end of week. Quietly, like she asked." A beat. "She meant what she said, by the way. About the distinction mattering." "I know she did." "Good." He tilts his head toward the door. "Get some sleep. You look like the Fragmentum won." —— Meanwhile, in the Underworld's outer machine district, a different kind of meeting is happening. The workshop belongs to Svarog, which means it is enormous, obsessively organized, and smells of machine oil and scorched metal in a ratio that Luka has always found oddly comforting. He grew up around this smell. It means work is getting done, and work getting done means problems getting solved, and Luka has always had more faith in solved problems than in most other things. He drops into the chair across the worktable from the massive automaton, who is currently disassembling something complicated and doing it the way Svarog does everything — with a focused, unhurried precision that makes Luka feel slightly chaotic by comparison, which is unfair because Svarog is twelve feet tall and has four arms. "You look like you haven't slept," Svarog observes, without looking up. "Everyone keeps saying that today." "Because it is true today." A pause, the clean sound of metal components separating. "You came from the surface." "Had a meeting." Luka stretches one arm over the back of the chair, his prosthetic resting on the table. "With the new Supreme Guardian. Up in the palace war room." Svarog is quiet for a moment. His optical sensors flicker, the subtle equivalent of a raised eyebrow. "The Silvermane Guard invited you to a war council." "The Supreme Guardian invited me to a war council. There's a distinction." He pauses, hearing Bronya's voice in his head as he says it, and finds it annoying that the distinction landed. "She wanted to know about surveillance in the outer tunnel networks. Whether we've got eyes down there." "Mmm." Svarog sets down the component he was working on and looks at Luka properly. His gaze, as always, has the quality of something that sees considerably more than is comfortable. "And you told her." "I told her I'd look into it." A pause. "Which means I'm asking you. Because you've got sensors in every corridor in the outer district that anyone with sense installed them in." "I installed them." "I know you installed them." "They are for monitoring structural integrity and atmosphere toxicity levels. Not surveillance." "Svarog." The automaton regards him for a long, deliberate moment. "The Fragmentum crystallization reached the outer tunnel wall in corridor seven-alpha four days ago," he says, with the calm of someone reporting something he has been waiting for someone to ask about. "Corridor nine-beta, six days ago. The growth rate is consistent with surface-level convergence patterns, which means it is not growing from inside the tunnels outward. It is growing from outside inward." A pause. "Something on the surface is pulling it." Luka stares at him. "You've known for six days." "I filed a structural integrity report with the Underworld administrative board." "The administrative board takes three weeks to process a structural integrity report." "Yes," Svarog says. "I am aware of this." And there is something in his voice — very faint, barely there — that sounds like frustration, which on Svarog is remarkable. He picks up the component again. "I was going to bring it to Natasha next. She has the Supreme Guardian's contact information." "I have the Supreme Guardian's contact information now," Luka says, and takes out his phone. "More specifically, I have Gepard's. Which is functionally the same thing apparently." He sends the message and puts the phone in his pocket, and they sit in the companionable near-silence of the workshop for a moment, the only sounds being the soft metallic work of Svarog's hands and the distant hum of the Underworld's generators. "You went to the surface for this meeting," Svarog says eventually. "Yeah." "Voluntarily." "She asked. It seemed important." He looks at his prosthetic, turning the wrist joint slowly. "It is important." "Mmm." Svarog works. "The silver-haired girl was also in the meeting." Luka goes very still. "How did you—" "You have a specific expression when you are trying not to think about something." Svarog does not look up. "You are currently making it." "I'm not—" He stops. Luka has known Svarog long enough to understand that arguing with him about observable data is a waste of both their time. "Seele is Bronya's sister. Obviously she was there." "Obviously," Svarog agrees, with the serenity of someone who has made his point and has no need to labor it. "She doesn't like me," Luka says, which comes out with slightly more feeling than intended. "She does not know you." "She's decided she doesn't like me, which in practice is the same thing." Svarog finally sets the component down and looks at him. His optical sensors carry, as they occasionally do, an expression that on a human face would be something like patience — the specific patience of someone watching a person be considerably more dense than they are capable of being about everything else. "You grew up on opposite sides of a wall that everyone told you was permanent," he says. "She is protective of what she nearly lost, and suspicious of what she does not yet understand. This is not the same as disliking you." Luka opens his mouth. Closes it. "The surveillance data," Svarog continues, returning to his work with perfect equanimity. "I will compile a full corridor report tonight. You can bring it to Commander Gepard in the morning." "Right." Luka looks at his phone. "Right. Yeah." The workshop hums. Somewhere deep in the outer district, a generator cycles through its nightly pressure check, the sound of it rolling through the tunnel walls like a slow mechanical tide. "Svarog," Luka says. "Yes." "Do you think it's going to get bad." Svarog is quiet for a moment. His four hands are still. "I think," he says carefully, "that it is already getting bad, and that the people on the surface are only now beginning to have the vocabulary for what they are looking at." He resumes his work. "Which means there is still time. But not, perhaps, as much as one would prefer." Luka nods. He looks at the corridor seven-alpha notation on his phone screen, at the date beside it — six days ago — and thinks about ten thousand people going about their lives in the amber warmth above him, and the thing moving quietly through the walls toward them, and the young woman in the palace war room with her silver hair and her too-careful posture who already knows and is trying to hold the line alone. He stays until Svarog finishes the report. Then he takes it, and heads toward the lift back to the surface. —— He does sleep. Four hours, total and dreamless, the kind of sleep that comes from a body collecting a debt owed in full. He wakes seven minutes before the evening patrol with the bone-deep punctuality that has lived in him since his first year in the Restricted Zone, takes a moment to lie in the dark and let the ceiling be the ceiling, and then gets up. The patrol takes four hours and is quiet. He is grateful for the quiet. He is less grateful for what the quiet does to his thoughts, which is give them room to move in directions he would prefer they didn't. He thinks about the residual Fragmentum distribution. The way it pointed, clearly and without ambiguity, toward the inhabited districts. The way Pela had said similar energy signature to Everwinter Hill and then stopped talking, and the way Bronya had not reacted to this like someone receiving new information. He thinks about what she said. I have a guess. But I don't want to say it until I'm sure. He has known Bronya since they were seventeen years old. He knows how she sounds when she is genuinely uncertain, and he knows how she sounds when she is certain and afraid of being right. In the outpost three nights ago, and in the council chamber this morning, and in the fragments she has been sending him at irregular hours — that was the second kind. He stands alone on the North ridge while his squad checks the convergence site below, and looks out at Belobog's outer wall.
