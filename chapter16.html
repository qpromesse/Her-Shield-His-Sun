Chapter 16: "What the Ground Holds"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text Gepard delivers what Natasha told him to Bronya at 1800 hours, after the IPC visitors have retired for the evening and the palace has settled into its quieter register. He gives it to her the way he gives her everything — in full, without softening, in order of importance. The dark winter. The targeted forgetting. The records purge classified as routine data management. The dying woman's words, which Natasha had carried for six years: it didn't feel like loss. It felt like being put to sleep. Bronya listens without interrupting, which is the thing she does when information is large enough to require the whole of her attention and she does not want to spend any part of it on words until she has the complete picture. When he finishes, she is quiet for a long moment. "The Surface Authority knew," she says. "Natasha believes so. The timing of the purge and its classification—" "They knew," she says again, more flatly. "And they purged the records. Not to protect the city from knowing about the Stellaron — the city didn't know about either Stellaron at that point. They purged the records to protect themselves from accountability for what happened to the outer machine district during the event." She looks at the desk. "Twenty-two years ago, a Stellaron activation injured or killed people in the Underworld's outer district, and the Surface Authority's response was to erase the documentation." "Yes," he says. She picks up her pen. She sets it down. "The people who did that are gone," she says. "The Surface Authority as it existed then is gone. Cocolia restructured it twice." She pauses. "That does not mean the people of the outer machine district don't deserve to know what happened to them." She looks at him. "We're going to tell them. When we understand enough of what happened to tell them accurately." "Yes," he says. She looks at the map on the wall — the one that has been there since the feedback loop crisis, the bloom sites still marked in faded amber, the outer district visible in the lower left corner. "Sampo," she says. "He grew up there," Gepard says. "He would have been approximately twelve during the dark winter. Old enough to have been affected. Old enough to have memories that were—" He pauses. "Taken," she says. "Yes." She is quiet again. He watches her think — the full-attention posture, everything directed inward, running the implications out to their edges. "He's known something was wrong for years," she says. "He came back to the district as an adult. He built his life there." She pauses. "On top of whatever he can't remember." "Yes." "He deserves to know." She says it the same way she said it about the outer district residents. Not a concession — a statement of fact. "Not at the end. As we find it." "Natasha asked me to tell you the same thing," he says. Something crosses her face — not surprise, something more like recognition, the expression of two people arriving at the same conclusion through different paths. "Of course she did," she says, quietly. Then: "I want to meet with Svarog." He looks at her. "I've been wanting to since before the IPC arrived," she says. "He's been in that district longer than the records go back. He has been watching Belobog from the ground level in a way that—" She pauses. "In a way that I haven't had access to. And if the second Stellaron is his district, if it has been below the place he has been guarding—" She looks at him. "I want to talk to him. Not as a data source. As—" She stops. "He's been protecting this city from below while the Silvermane Guard has been protecting it from above. That matters." She pauses. "He matters." Gepard looks at her — at the expression she is wearing, which is not the Guardian's expression and not quite the other face either. Something in between, the face of someone feeling the weight of a responsibility she has taken seriously since the day she sat on a throne she didn't want and chose to deserve it. "I'll arrange it," he says. "Tomorrow," she says. "After the morning session." She picks up the pen again. "And I want Pela's correlation analysis as soon as it's ready." "She said two more days." "Tell her one." She looks up. "Please." He almost smiles. "I'll tell her." —— Pela's reaction to being told one day instead of two is to look up from her analysis with the expression she wears when someone has said something she is technically willing to accept and practically finds inconvenient, which is the only expression of mild disagreement available to Pela, who does not do larger expressions of inconvenience. "One day," she says. "She said please," Gepard says. Pela returns to her analysis. "One day," she says. —— The morning session on day four of the IPC visit belongs, unexpectedly, to Aventurine. He arrives at the council chamber at 0900 with a folder that is thinner than anything Topaz has produced, which means either it is a summary or it is something he has been saving, and sits at the table not in his previous position but in the chair closer to the window, which puts him in the morning light and puts everyone else slightly against it, which Gepard notes as deliberate. "I'd like to discuss the second Stellaron's location," he says, before anyone else speaks. Bronya looks at him. "You'd like to confirm it," she says. "You already have a location estimate from your external readings." "I'd like to discuss access," he says. "To the corridor network in the outer machine district." He pauses. "Specifically, I'd like to discuss what's past junction eight of corridor nine-beta." The room is quiet. Gepard looks at Seele. Seele's expression does not change, but something in her posture adjusts — the very slight recalibration of someone who was not expecting that specific junction to be named. "You've been past junction eight," Bronya says. "My associate has been past junction eight," Aventurine says, which means Chen, who walked with him yesterday. Gepard makes a note to talk to Chen. "And," Bronya says. "And the readings past junction eight are significantly different from the readings in the first eight junctions." He opens his folder. "I have a preliminary energy profile from yesterday's walk. The directional signature of the second Stellaron's output is consistent with a source approximately forty meters below the surface of junction nine's floor." He slides the page across. "Which means the second Stellaron's chamber is accessible — potentially — through the corridor network past junction eight, if you go deep enough and far enough." He looks at Bronya. "Which means the IPC's assessment of the second Stellaron cannot be completed from the surface level." Bronya picks up the page. She looks at it for a moment. "You want access to the corridor network," she says. "I want to propose a joint survey," he says. "IPC equipment, Belobog personnel, corridor network past junction eight." He pauses. "Together." "Joint," Topaz says, which means she has reviewed this proposal and agreed to it, which means it was discussed last night, which means they have been planning this since before they walked the outer machine district. Bronya looks at Gepard. He looks back at her with the expression that says: I don't have enough to tell you what the right answer is yet, which is a version of his expression she has learned to read over the past few months. She looks at the page. "I'll give you an answer this afternoon," she says. Aventurine nods. "Of course," he says, with the ease of someone who expected exactly this. —— After the session, Seele finds Luka. This is not difficult — he is in the outer machine district, which is where he always is when he is not somewhere else, and the district is not large enough for finding to take long. She walks into Svarog's workshop without knocking, which she has done exactly once before and which Luka, at the second workbench repairing something with Svarog's tools, looks up at with an expression that starts at neutral and crosses through something that is not quite surprise. "Aventurine wants access to the corridor past junction eight," she says, without preamble. "I heard," he says. "How did you hear." "Clara told me." He gestures, slightly, toward the far corner of the workshop where Clara is seated on a high stool at the edge of Svarog's storage shelves, working on something small and intricate that involves a soldering iron and the intense focus of a person doing a delicate task. She is small in the workshop — twelve years old in a space built for a four-armed automaton, perched on the stool with her feet not quite reaching the floor, and the specific quality of her concentration is the concentration of a child who has learned to do careful things, not the focused attention of an adult who chooses careful work. She does not look up. She is at the part of the task that does not have room for looking up. "Aventurine told her this morning," Luka says. "When he came to return Svarog's data chip." "He returned it." "Copied it first, obviously. But he returned it." Luka sets down the tool he was using. "Clara, Aventurine said the readings past eight were — what was the word he used." "Remarkable," Clara says, to the soldering iron, in the tone of someone quoting a word they found interesting and have been turning over since. She pauses her work for a moment, the way children pause things they are deeply concentrating on when an adult asks them a direct question. "He said remarkable and then he said something in a language I don't recognize and I asked him what it meant and he said he didn't know either, someone taught it to him once." She returns to her work. "I thought that was a strange thing to say." Seele looks at her. At the small figure on the high stool, at the focused bent head and the careful hands. Something about Clara in this workshop — something about the scale of her, the child-size of her in the large space — pulls at something Seele does not have a name for, something that lives in the part of her that does not have names for things yet. She looks away. She looks at Luka. "Aventurine went past junction eight yesterday," she says. "With Chen," Luka says. "Chen is a Silvermane Guard soldier and not equipped for what's past eight." She looks at him. "We found a Chrysalis form past eight yesterday. They didn't." "Different time of day. Different proximity to the source." He picks up the tool again. "The Chrysalis form responded to you specifically. Chen wouldn't have triggered it." She looks at him. "You're saying the Chrysalis was responding to me." "I said that in the tunnel." "You said it oriented on me. You didn't say it was responding to me specifically as opposed to just targeting the nearest warm body." He looks at her. "The torch was the nearest warm body by thermal signature and the torch was in my hand. It ignored the torch." He pauses. "It oriented on you. That's a distinction." She holds his gaze. He holds hers. The workshop is warm from the soldering iron and smells of metal and machine oil and the faint electric ozone of geomarrow-adjacent work, and between them the thing that has been building has its usual quality of pull-against-resistance — she can feel both simultaneously, always, the pull that has no explanation and the resistance that has all of them. He has never earned her trust. He was given a job by Sampo and turned up in her tunnels and started going four times a week when he was told twice, and none of that is the same as trust. And yet. "You think the second Stellaron is responding to me specifically," she says. "Not to both of us. Me." "I think it's responding to both of us," he says. "But differently. You're a different kind of variable than I am." "Why." "I don't know yet," he says, which at least is honest. "But the Chrysalis behavior and the way the heartbeat gets louder when you're in proximity—" He stops. He looks at the tool in his hand. "It's louder when you're in the tunnel than when I'm alone." She stares at him. "You've been comparing." "I log everything," he says. "You've been logging the relative intensity of the— the sound — between solo visits and visits when I'm present." "Yes." She looks at him with the expression that is not the hostile wariness but is something sharper — the look she gives things she finds genuinely offensive, not because they are bad but because they are presumptuous. "That is an enormous amount of data to be collecting about me without telling me." "I'm telling you now." "You're telling me now because it's relevant to the Aventurine conversation, not because you decided transparency was appropriate." She crosses her arms. "You've been running analysis on my response to the second Stellaron without my knowledge." He looks at her directly. "Yes," he says. "I have. Because the data exists in the logs and it's relevant and I don't throw away relevant data." He pauses. "I didn't tell you because until this week I didn't have enough to say anything useful and I thought you'd tell me to stop logging it." "I would have told you to stop logging it." "I know," he says. "That's why I didn't tell you." The workshop is quiet for a moment. Svarog is in the back room — they can hear the distant sound of his work — and Clara is very carefully not participating in this conversation while remaining fully present to it, the specific attention of a twelve-year-old who has learned that adults sometimes need to be heard without being interrupted and who is managing this by focusing very hard on her soldering. "You're going to keep logging it," Seele says. "Yes," he says. "Even though I'm telling you I find it—" "Presumptuous," he says. "Yes. Because the data matters more than whether you find my methods presumptuous." He meets her gaze. "And because you know it matters more. You're angry about the presumption, not about the logging itself." She stares at him. He looks back at her with the straightforwardness that is simply his face, the one that does not perform neutrality and does not apologize for its own assessments, and she hates that he is correct, and she hates that she can tell he knows she knows he is correct, and she hates that none of this changes the fact that the data he has been quietly collecting about her response to a twenty-two-year-old Stellaron activation is going to be relevant in the next week in ways she cannot predict. "Fine," she says, which is the specific word she uses when she has decided to accept something she does not like rather than waste time arguing against a correct position. "Log it. But you share every finding with me in real time. No more deciding when it's useful enough to mention." He looks at her. Something in his expression shifts — not the easy straightforwardness, something slightly surprised at the edges, the expression of someone who conceded a point they expected to have to fight for and has found the ground give more quickly than anticipated. "Alright," he says. "And I want to see the existing logs. All of them. Including the comparative intensity data." "I'll send them tonight." "Now," she says. He looks at her. She looks at him. He takes out his phone and sends them. Her phone buzzes. She does not look at it. She keeps looking at him. He keeps looking at her. And the thing between them has its usual quality — the pull and the resistance simultaneously, the attraction that has no explanation fighting the hostility that has all of them, and neither of them acknowledges either. "The corridor past eight," she says. "Aventurine wants access. Bronya is deciding this afternoon." She pauses. "Whatever she decides, I'm going back in." She looks at him. "Are you." "Yes," he says. "With better equipment." "Svarog is already building it," he says. She looks toward the back room — toward the sound of Svarog working. "He's building it now? Today?" "He started last night after I told him about the Chrysalis." Luka pauses. "He said: I have been waiting for someone to need this equipment for a long time." He looks at the back room. "He didn't explain what he meant by that." "No," Seele says. "He wouldn't." Clara looks up from her soldering, briefly — the quick upward glance of a child who has been listening carefully and has heard something that interests her. "Svarog built equipment for the tunnels before," she says. "A long time ago. Before I was built." She looks at Seele. "He kept it in the storage section at the back. He never said why." She looks back at her work. "I asked him once. He said it was for when it was needed." A pause, the deliberate pause of a twelve-year-old who knows she has said something significant and is choosing to leave it at its own weight. "I thought that was a strange thing to keep." Seele and Luka look at each other. "Show me the storage section," Seele says, to Clara. "Svarog would have to," Clara says. "It's his." She hops off the stool with the particular combination of carefulness and energy that belongs to children doing things they are not fully sure they have permission for. "I'll go ask him." —— Svarog's storage section contains, among other things, equipment that was built for a specific purpose and has been waiting for the occasion of that purpose for a very long time. He presents it to them without ceremony — three cases, older than any equipment in the workshop by decades, built to a specification that is not modern and does not need to be, because what they were built to measure has not changed in twenty-two years. Directional sensors with a range Luka's recalibrated secondary module cannot approach. Shielding components designed for sustained proximity to an active Stellaron source. And one additional item, which Svarog sets on the worktable with the four-armed care he gives important things: a frequency recorder, designed to capture resonance signatures in ranges far below what standard equipment catches. "Below what any standard equipment catches," Luka says, looking at it. "Yes," Svarog says. "Because the second Stellaron's output is in that range." "The output that is audible to — certain people," Svarog says, which is as close as he will come, today, to naming them directly. His optical sensors rest on Seele for a moment. She meets his gaze. "The recorder will capture what you can hear," he says to her. "And give it a form that others can analyze." She looks at the recorder. It is old. The casing has the look of something that has been maintained rather than replaced, kept functional through decades of care. "You built this for me," she says. Svarog is quiet for a moment. "I built it for whoever would need it," he says. "I hoped it would be you." He pauses. "I have been hoping that for some time." Clara, standing at the edge of the worktable, looks at the recorder with the expression children have when they are in the presence of something they sense matters without fully understanding why — serious, attentive, a little wide-eyed. She looks at Svarog. She looks at the recorder. She does not ask the question she is clearly holding, which is a significant act of restraint for a twelve-year-old, and which she is managing by keeping both hands very carefully in her pockets. "Can I see how it works," she asks, and immediately: "Later. Not now. When it's not important." Svarog looks at her. The expression his optical sensors carry sometimes, the one that on a human face would be called fond, is present in a way that is not subtle. "Yes," he says. "Later." —— Bronya gives Aventurine her answer at 1400 hours. She gives it in the office, with Gepard present, which Aventurine clearly anticipated because he arrives without Topaz, which means this is not the IPC's official negotiating posture but something more direct. "Joint survey," she says. "IPC equipment, Belobog personnel, coordinated entry." She pauses. "The corridor network past junction eight is not a controlled environment. Yesterday there was an active Chrysalis form past that point in a section that has been reading dormant for weeks." She lets this land. "Which means the second Stellaron's increasing activity is producing Fragmentum responses in its immediate proximity. Which means entering that corridor is not a documentation exercise — it is a hazard." Aventurine's expression is doing the work it does. "I'm aware of the Chrysalis," he says. "Chen told me this morning." "I know he did," she says. "Because I told him to." She meets Aventurine's gaze. "I'm not keeping information from you as a negotiating position. I'm giving you the complete picture so that the joint survey is actually joint — not the IPC's assessment with Belobog personnel as an escort." She pauses. "The people who know those corridors are mine. The people who can hear the second Stellaron's signal are mine. The equipment Svarog has been building for twenty-two years is mine." She pauses. "Your external monitoring data and your analytical framework are yours. Those are the terms of joint." Aventurine looks at her for a long moment. The entertained expression is absent. He is looking at her the way he looked at the first Stellaron in the chamber — with the stripped attention, the unperformed version. "You've been busy today," he says. "I'm always busy," she says. He is quiet for another moment. Then: "Agreed," he says. "Those terms." "Good." She looks at the desk. "One more condition." "Of course," he says. "Sampo Koski is part of the team entering the corridor." She looks up. "Not as IPC support or Belobog intelligence asset. As someone with a personal stake in what the second Stellaron is holding." She holds his gaze. "He grew up in the outer machine district. Whatever happened twenty-two years ago happened to him too. He is going in with eyes open and full information, or he is not going in." Aventurine looks at her. "You know," he says, quietly. "I know some of it," she says. "Sampo doesn't know any of it. I'm changing that today." She pauses. "Those are the terms." Aventurine is quiet for a long moment. He looks at the window. He looks back at her. He looks at Gepard — not the reading-the-room look, something more direct, something that is actually asking. Gepard meets it with the expression that says: she means it, and she's right. Not the performance. Not the stripped version, the one that appears in serious moments. Sampo's face does something complicated and private when he hears it, the way it always does when it registers her proximity, except with more of the layers stripped away than usual.
