Chapter 32: "Sub-Level Two"

Chapter Text Sub-level two begins with the smell. Luka notices it at the passage entrance — the passage from sub-level one's cave floor down to sub-level two, which Svarog's equipment mapped as a natural formation rather than a constructed one, the bedrock having split along a fault line at some point in the geological past and the split running downward at an angle that is not quite a corridor and not quite a shaft but something between the two, wide enough to descend through with care. The smell that reaches them at the passage entrance is not the machine oil and cold metal of the outer machine district's upper tunnels. It is older than that. Deeper. The cold here is not the textural cold of the outer corridors — it is the cold of somewhere that has never in its geological history been warm, the cold of bedrock at depth that has been sealed from the surface for longer than the Underworld has existed. He logs it. He does not say this out loud. He logs it because everything gets logged and because the smell is information — it tells him the depth is genuine, that sub-level two is not an extension of the human-built infrastructure but a natural space that the Stellaron's expansion has reached and which the Stellaron-proximate Fragmentum has been filling for twenty-two years. Gepard is at the passage entrance beside him. Seele is behind him with the scythe at her hip and the frequency recorder on its chest mount and the shielding component calibrated to Svarog's specification for sub-level two's density. Serval is behind Seele with the upgraded recorder and the portable analyzer and the specific quality she has had since the outer corridor seven discovery — the quality of someone who knows there is a third sub-level below this and is running the analysis on this sub-level with that knowledge in the back of her calculations. Aventurine is not there. He sent a message the previous evening: 'I'm on a transport back to the IPC's regional office. Topaz needs the preliminary documentation filed before the end of the week and it requires both of us. The Cornerstone is available — I'll send the operating instructions. The field radius extends to anyone within three meters of the active unit.' He'll understand them.' Gepard had read the operating instructions. He had understood them. The Cornerstone is on his chest mount — not Aventurine's style of carrying it, which was the casual confident positioning of someone who has operated the device for years, but the careful positioning of someone who has read the instructions once and is prepared to be rigorous about the deployment. The field radius. The efficiency degradation per impact. The specific deployment trigger. He has run the calculation for sub-level two's estimated form density. He has a number. The number is not comfortable. He has not told Luka and Seele the number. He will tell them if it becomes relevant. At the moment, it is information about his own operational constraints and does not require advance broadcast. "Passage," he says. "Ready," Seele says. "Ready," Luka says. Serval says: "The recorder is running from entry. The harmonic is already elevated above sub-level one's baseline reading. Whatever is down here is more active than sub-level one was at entry." She pauses. "I want the full capture from first contact. Don't wait for the contact event to tell me — call it the moment it begins." "Yes," Luka says, and this time it is not the one-word response in the flattening register — it is the yes that means understood and I will because you asked me to and I know why it matters. They go through the passage. —— Sub-level two is not a cave. Sub-level one was a cave — the natural geological formation, the organic crystal growth, the irregular floor. Sub-level two is something else. The descent through the passage brings them into a space that has the quality of something deliberately shaped — not carved like the outer corridor's pre-Separation construction, not constructed like nine-beta's tunnel network, but shaped by forces that were deliberate rather than random. The walls are smooth where sub-level one's walls were rough. The ceiling is consistent — nine meters, as Svarog's notes predicted — and the floor is flat, almost perfectly flat, the bedrock here having been leveled by the Stellaron's expansion in the way that extreme heat levels stone. The Stellaron's expansion has been working on sub-level two for twenty-two years. The result is a space that has the quality of being prepared. The old gold and deep water light is not ambient here — it is directional. The light comes from below, from the floor itself, conducting upward through the stone the way heat conducts, and the effect is that the space is lit from underneath, all the shadows pointing upward, the geometry inverted from every other light source Luka has ever been in proximity to. "The Stellaron's surface is below the floor here," Serval says, reading the deep-range array before Luka can. "The expansion boundary has reached the sub-level two floor and is pressing upward against it rather than expanding laterally. The floor is—" She pauses. "The floor is the direct contact surface. The entire floor." "Not a light pool," Luka says. "The whole floor." "Yes," she says. "The contact event here is going to be—" She pauses again, and the pause has the quality that Svarog's four-second pause had. "Different from sub-level one." Seele is looking at the floor.
