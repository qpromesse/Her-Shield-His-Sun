Chapter 5: "Resonance"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text Pela's research hits its first wall on day six. She sends the update at 11 PM, which means she has been working since before the Sector C bloom and has not stopped, which means she has been awake for approximately twenty hours, which means the update is probably accurate and probably also missing things she would have caught at hour twelve. Gepard reads it at the Sector B observation post, standing under the cable spool array with his pauldron charging and his phone in his hand, and reads it twice. The summary is this: resonance dampening is theoretically viable. The pre-Separation research records confirm it has been done before — once, in a controlled environment, with equipment that no longer exists, by researchers whose methodology is described in terms that assume a baseline of knowledge Pela is currently reconstructing from fragments. The interrupting frequency is calculable. The delivery mechanism is not. The only confirmed method in the historical record required a geomarrow source of magnitude comparable to the Stellaron itself, channeled through a purpose-built array that took two years to construct and is now, presumably, somewhere under forty meters of Fragmentum-saturated permafrost. Thirteen days. He sends a reply that says: keep going, and puts the phone away. —— In the Underworld's outer corridor seven-alpha, Luka is doing something he told Sampo he would think about and has apparently finished thinking about. He moves through the tunnel with a handheld sensor Svarog modified for him — a standard atmospheric toxicity reader with a secondary crystallization detection module soldered to its side in a way that is not factory specification but works better than factory specification, which is Svarog's approach to most problems. The reading climbs steadily as he moves deeper into the outer corridor, the amber indicator light on the sensor shifting from yellow to a pale, persistent orange that he does not find comforting. The crystallization on the walls here is not the dramatic spire formations he's seen in salvage runs near the surface. It is thin. Almost delicate. A film of amber-black threading through the concrete like roots, following the seams between construction blocks, branching at junctions in patterns that his eye keeps trying to read as deliberate. The tunnel smells of cold stone and something underneath the cold stone — something with a low, metallic edge that is not quite any smell he knows and that his body responds to with a very specific and unhelpful variety of alertness. He photographs everything. He logs the sensor readings at every junction marker. He moves carefully and without noise, because Svarog told him to and because the tunnel has the quality of a space that is listening, which is an irrational thought he has anyway and which he does not write down in his log because Sampo will read it and say something. At junction seven-alpha-four, he stops. The crystallization on the north wall is different from the rest. It is not threading through the seams. It is growing over them — a solid, continuous sheet of amber-black, perhaps two meters wide, that has covered the construction markings entirely and is pressing outward from the wall surface in a slow, almost imperceptible convexity. He puts his hand near it, not touching, and the sensor in his other hand spikes into the red. He photographs it from four angles. He logs the sensor reading. He takes a step back. The crystallization does not move. He takes another step back. The amber light in the sheet pulses once — a single, slow rhythm, like a heartbeat, like a breath — and then is still. Luka stands in the cold of corridor seven-alpha-four for a moment longer, his sensor still reading red, his photograph log open, and he makes a decision that Sampo is going to tell him was unnecessary and Svarog is going to say nothing about while clearly thinking it was correct. He marks the junction on his map. He notates the pulse. He keeps going. —— The structural assessment of the Sector C wall comes back at noon on day seven with numbers that Gepard carries to Bronya's office and sets on her desk without preamble, because preamble would be a way of softening them and he has decided not to do that. She reads them. She sets the document down. She looks at the wall-mounted map with the bloom sites marked in amber. "The east wall can hold another impact at that force level," she says. "One. Maybe two, if the bloom growth rate stays below the Sector C peak." He sits down in the chair that has become, over the past week, functionally his chair — the one pulled from the corner, positioned across from her desk at an angle that is neither purely professional nor anything that could be called otherwise. "If the growth rate accelerates, one." "And Pela's dampening timeline." "She's working on the delivery mechanism. The theory is sound. The engineering—" He pauses. "She needs more time than we have." Bronya is quiet. Outside the office window, the pale northern light sits on the rooftops of Belobog's upper city, indifferent and cold. He watches her look at the map and thinks — again, always — about what it costs to sit in that chair and hold all of this. The Stellaron's voice. The feedback loop. The wall with one impact left in it. Thirteen days on Pela's conservative estimate, which is now twelve, and the Fragmentum learning their patrol schedules faster than they can change them. She is twenty-two years old. He has known her since she was seventeen, since the first time she stood at the front of a briefing room and told a group of soldiers twice her age exactly what they were doing wrong and exactly how to fix it, and the certainty in her voice was so complete that no one questioned it, and she was right. He had thought at the time that this was the most remarkable thing he had ever seen. He has been revising this assessment upward every year since. "Pela needs another approach," Bronya says. "A secondary option. Something that doesn't require equipment we don't have." She picks up a pen. "I've been looking at the pre-Separation records myself. There's a reference in the Architect's original survey — fragmentary, barely legible — to a technique called fractured resonance. Not dampening. Disruption." She writes something, slides the paper across the desk to him. "Instead of generating a counter-frequency to neutralize the signal, you introduce a dissonant frequency to fracture it. Like—" "Like breaking a harmonic," he says, reading. "Yes. The energy required is lower. The geomarrow source doesn't need to match the Stellaron's magnitude — it needs to be precisely calibrated to the dissonant interval." She pauses. "Which is a much harder calibration problem. But it's a solvable one." He looks at the equations she's written. They are, to his eye, significantly above his level of technical comprehension, but the shape of the argument is clear enough. "Does Pela know about this." "I sent it to her this morning. She's been looking at the same records, but from the dampening angle. This reframes the problem." She looks at him. "It might buy us time we don't otherwise have." "How much time do you need." "To verify the calibration and build the delivery mechanism?" She looks at the ceiling briefly. "If Pela has help and the right materials — five days. Six if something goes wrong." "We have twelve." "Then we have room." "If nothing else goes wrong." She looks at him. Her eyes are very level. "Yes," she says. "If nothing else goes wrong." He nods. He slides the paper back. He does not say what they are both thinking, which is that something else going wrong is not a question of whether but of when, because the Fragmentum has been wrong-footing them consistently for a week and there is no particular reason to believe it has exhausted its capacity to do so. "I'll need materials from the Energy Hub," she says. "Geomarrow grade-three or higher, at least four units. And I'll need Serval." He blinks. "Serval," he says. "She built the Energy Hub's current resonance array. She understands geomarrow harmonics better than anyone in Belobog." A pause. "Better than anyone I know of anywhere." He thinks about his sister. He thinks about the way she had looked, the last time he saw her, three weeks ago — tired but animated, the way she always is when she has a problem to work on, which is most of the time. He thinks about asking her to help calibrate a dissonant frequency to interrupt a Stellaron broadcasting a signal that is drawing Fragmentum toward a city of ten thousand people. "She'll say yes," he says. "I know." A pause. "That's not what I'm asking you." He looks at her. She is looking at the map, her pen tapping once against the desk, and he understands. She is not asking whether Serval will agree. She is asking whether he will ask her — whether he is willing to bring his sister into this. "I'll call her this afternoon," he says. Bronya looks at him. The fortress-hands posture loosens, just slightly, at the wrists. "Thank you." "Don't thank me until it works." "I'll thank you now," she says, with the precision of someone who has learned not to defer gratitude on the assumption that there will be a better moment later. "And again if it works." He stands. He picks up his coat. He is almost to the door when she says, without looking up from the map: "Gepard." "Yes." "The phone call last week. At 2 AM." A pause, the pen tapping once. "I've been thinking about what you said." He waits. "About concern being different from fear." She is still looking at the map. "I've been trying to practice that distinction." "How is it going." "Poorly," she says, which is the most honest thing she has said to him in a week, and he receives it accordingly. "Keep practicing," he says. He goes. —— Serval picks up on the second ring. "Gep." Her voice has the specific warmth of someone who is always genuinely pleased when he calls and has never been particularly restrained about showing it, which is one of approximately ten thousand ways she is his opposite. "I was just thinking about you. I found a photo from your first year in the Guard and you look absolutely — anyway. What's up." "I have a job for you," he says. "It's important." A pause. He can hear her reading the register of his voice. She has always been good at this — better than most people, which given what she does for a living makes sense. "How important." "Stellaron-level important." Silence. "Gep." "I need your help with a resonance problem. Geomarrow harmonic calibration. Bronya asked for you specifically — she said you built the Energy Hub array." "I designed the frequency architecture, yeah." Her voice has changed — not alarmed, exactly, but focused, the way it gets when a problem has her full attention. "What kind of resonance problem." He tells her. He gives her the version he would give anyone he trusts completely with technical information, which is the accurate version, which includes the Stellaron broadcasting, the feedback loop, the fractured resonance approach Bronya identified, and the twelve-day window. He gives it to her the way he gives Bronya her reports — without softening, without speculation, just the shape of the thing clearly described. When he finishes, Serval is quiet for a moment. "The dissonant interval calibration," she says. "That's the hard part. You need to know the Stellaron's exact broadcast frequency first, or you're calculating blind." "Bronya can give you the frequency. She's been hearing it." Another pause. Longer this time. "She's been — Gep. She's been hearing the Stellaron." "For two months." "And she's been—" He can hear Serval doing several calculations at once, the way she does, and then arriving at the one that matters. "She's okay." "She's managing." "That's not—" "I know," he says. "She's Bronya." A silence that has a very specific texture to it — the texture of two people who both know someone extremely well and are sharing, without saying it, a particular variety of worry that is inseparable from a particular variety of respect. Then Serval exhales. "When do you need me." "Tomorrow, if you can." "I'll be there tonight." A pause. "And Gep? The photo. You really did look absolutely terrible. You had this expression like someone had personally wronged you by asking you to stand still for a camera." "Serval." "I'm just saying. Some things don't change." The warmth is back in her voice, fully, unreservedly. "I'll see you tonight." She hangs up. He puts his phone in his pocket and stands in the corridor outside Bronya's office for a moment longer, listening to the palace settle around him — the distant sounds of the administrative wing, the muffled voices, the cold northern light coming through the high windows. He thinks about Serval's voice when he said she's been hearing the Stellaron, and the specific quality of that pause, and decides that he will not examine it too closely because examining it too closely means acknowledging something he is not ready to acknowledge in the middle of a twelve-day window with a stressed perimeter wall and a Fragmentum that is learning. He files it. He goes to find Pela. —— In the Underworld, Sampo delivers Luka's first tunnel report to Natasha's ward in person rather than by message, partly because the ward is on his way to the Tier Two bakery and partly because the report contains the notation about the pulse in corridor seven-alpha-four, and he wants to read her face when she reads it. She reads it at the front desk, standing, chart forgotten in her other hand. He watches her expression do the thing it does with information she doesn't like — a very controlled, very thorough stillness, the way a surface goes still when something is moving underneath it. "He saw it pulse," she says. "Once. Single rhythm. Then nothing." "That's not ambient crystallization behavior." "No," Sampo agrees. "It's not." She looks at the report for another moment. Then she sets it on the desk and looks at him — fully, the way she rarely looks at him when they are both aware that other people might see, the way that makes him feel, in a register he has learned not to examine at close range, like she is reading something he has not said. "You sent a twenty-year-old into a corridor with active crystallization," she says. "He's twenty-one. And he volunteered." "Sampo." "He went to Svarog first. He had modified equipment. He knows those tunnels better than the sensor maps." He meets her gaze, which is steadier than his. "I didn't send him anywhere he couldn't handle." "You don't know that." "I know Svarog wouldn't have let him go if he couldn't." She holds his gaze for a moment. He holds hers. This is a thing they do sometimes — this specific variety of standoff that is not actually a standoff because neither of them is angry, it is more like two people verifying each other's coordinates, making sure they are still pointing in compatible directions. She looks away first, which she almost never does. "Send him to me when he's done with the next walkthrough," she says. "I want to check him over." "He'll say he's fine." "He will. Send him anyway." "Natasha." She looks at him. He picks up the report and taps the junction notation. "The pulse. You know what it means." She is quiet. "The Fragmentum down here is connected to whatever is happening on the surface," he says. "Not just growing toward it. Communicating with it." He sets the report down. "Which means the tunnels are not a secondary vector. They're part of the same system." "I know," she says. "Pela needs to know." "I know." "So does—" "I know, Sampo." Her voice is not sharp, but it is final. She picks the report back up, and her hands are very steady. "I'll send it up today." A pause. "Both the finding and the implication." He looks at her. In the ward behind her, someone coughs. A door opens and closes. The ordinary sounds of a medical facility doing its ordinary work, sitting on top of something that is not ordinary at all. "Are you alright," he says, and he means it the way he always means it with her — stripped of any theater, just the question. She looks at him for a moment. "No," she says, which is the answer she only gives him, and which he receives accordingly. "But I will be. I'm always alright eventually." "I know," he says. "I'll bring the dark filling ones tomorrow." The corner of her mouth moves. Not quite a smile, but in the same neighborhood. "You don't have to." "I know." He picks up his coat. At the door he pauses, because something makes him pause — some instinct that has kept him alive through years of operating in spaces where instincts are the difference — and he turns back. "The pulse in seven-alpha-four," he says. "The single rhythm." He looks at her. "How long has it been doing that." She meets his gaze. Her hands are very still on the report. "I don't know," she says. He nods. He goes. —— Serval arrives at the palace at nine in the evening with a bag of equipment, six pages of frequency calculations she did on the train, and the particular energy of someone who has been running on scientific curiosity and personal loyalty since the moment she hung up the phone. Gepard meets her at the service entrance, because she does not have palace credentials and he has been managing the quiet circulation of unofficial visitors through the service entrance for two months now and has gotten efficient at it. She hugs him in the corridor, which he permits — he permits it specifically because she is Serval and because he has not seen her in three weeks and because the last time he went three weeks without seeing her before that was the year he spent his first rotation at the forward Restricted Zone post at nineteen years old, and the year after that she had shown up at the post with food and frequency calculations and complained at length about the structural inadequacy of his living quarters. Some things don't change. "You look tired," she says, pulling back and looking at him with the unguarded critical affection of a sibling. "You look like you did frequency calculations on a train." "I did frequency calculations on a train. They're good ones." She holds up the pages. "Where's Bronya." "Office. Second floor." She is already moving. He falls into step beside her. The palace corridor is quiet at this hour — the administrative shift long ended, most of the palace staff gone for the night, only the overnight guard rotation moving through the outer rings. Their footsteps echo. "Gep," Serval says, without looking at him. "Yes." "Two months." He knows what she means. He says nothing. "You didn't tell me she was hearing the Stellaron." "I didn't know you needed to know." "She is our Supreme Guardian and also a person I care about very much and also—" She stops. Starts again. "You could have told me." "She asked me not to tell people until she was certain." "And you—" Serval exhales. He can hear her making the decision to let it go, which costs her something, which he appreciates. "Right. Of course you did." A pause. "Is she okay." "She's managing." "Gepard." "She's Bronya," he says, which is the same answer he gave an hour ago on the phone, and which means the same thing now. Serval is quiet for a moment. He glances at her — at the way she is looking at the corridor ahead of them, the frequency pages in her hand, her brow slightly furrowed in the way that means she is processing something she is not going to say out loud right now and will probably bring up later at a significantly worse time. "What," he says. "Nothing." "Serval." "I said nothing." She glances at him sideways, and the corner of her mouth does something he recognizes — the specific compression of a smile being suppressed, the one that means she knows something and is deciding whether now is the time. She decides it isn't. "Later," she says. He files this under things that will come back to inconvenience him and does not push. The office door is open, which means Bronya heard them coming, or has been leaving it open, or both. She looks up when they appear in the doorway, and her expression does something he does not see often — a brief, complete, unguarded thing, the kind that happens when a face isn't ready for the room. Serval in the doorway. Gepard beside her. The frequency pages. "You came tonight," Bronya says. "He said twelve days," Serval says, crossing the room and dropping into the chair across from the desk with the ease of someone who has never once in her life stood on ceremony. "Show me the records." Bronya looks at her for a moment. Then she opens the desk drawer and produces a folder — the pre-Separation research records, the fractured resonance reference, the calculations she slid across the desk to Gepard that morning. She slides them across to Serval. Serval reads. The office is quiet. Gepard takes up his position near the window. Outside, Belobog's upper city is dark and cold and entirely unaware of the three people in this room and the thing they are attempting to build an answer to. "The dissonant interval," Serval says, still reading. "You're right, it's a calibration problem, not a magnitude problem." She looks up. "I need the broadcast frequency. Exactly." Bronya meets her eyes. "I know," she says. And then, with the careful precision of someone who has been measuring a thing for weeks and is only now saying the measurement out loud: "It's 4.7 kilocycles per Belobog standard second. With a harmonic overtone at 14.1." A pause. "It's been climbing. It went up by 0.3 cycles in the last week." Serval stares at her. "You've been measuring it," she says. "I've been hearing it," Bronya says. "I started mapping it when I realized it was changing." Serval looks at Gepard. He looks back. The specific texture of that glance — what it contains, what it asks, what it doesn't say — is something he will think about later, in the outpost at 2 AM, with more honesty than he currently has access to. "Okay," Serval says, and looks back at the pages. "Okay. Give me six hours." She starts working. Bronya makes tea. Gepard charges his armor. The office settles into the particular quiet of people doing focused work in the same space — the scratch of Serval's pen, the soft hum of the geomarrow in the walls, the distant sound of the Restricted Zone's night patrol calling in its first hourly check through the hardwired terminal two floors up. At some point past midnight, Gepard looks up from the patrol report he has been reading to find Bronya asleep in her chair, her tea half-finished, her head listing slightly to one side, the map on the wall in front of her still marked with its amber bloom sites and its fourteen-day clock. He does not wake her. Serval looks up from her calculations, takes in the scene, and looks at Gepard with an expression he has no immediate defense against — the one that is not quite a smile and not quite a question, that simply says: I see you. He looks back at his patrol report. "Gep," she says, very quietly. "Don't," he says, equally quietly. She makes the suppressed-smile expression again. She goes back to her calculations. Outside, the snow falls on Belobog. Inside, in the pale lamp-light of the second floor office, the fractured resonance solution takes shape one equation at a time, and the man near the window reads his patrol report three more times without retaining a word of it, and does not examine why. —— Notes: Luka saw the pulse in corridor seven-alpha-four and wrote it down anyway because he is built different, I said what I said.
