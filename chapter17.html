Chapter 17: "In"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text They go in on a Thursday morning at 0700. The group is larger than any previous tunnel entry: Gepard, Seele, Luka, Sampo, and Aventurine, with Svarog's equipment distributed between them. Topaz does not come — she stays above with Pela to continue the documentation review, which is both practical and, Gepard suspects, a calculated choice on Aventurine's part to have his most analytically precise colleague positioned where she can receive data in real time rather than generate it in a cave. Bronya does not come either, which costs her something visible when she says it. She says it in the office the night before, to Gepard, looking at the map with the outer district marked in the lower left corner. "I should be there," she says. "You're the Supreme Guardian of Belobog and the IPC's official host," he says. "If something goes wrong in that corridor—" "If something goes wrong in that corridor I want to be in it, not receiving reports about it." She turns from the map. The lamp catches her face and she looks, briefly, twenty-two years old and carrying something too large for twenty-two and doing it anyway, the way she always does. "Don't give me the Guardian argument. Give me the real one." He looks at her. "The real one is that if both of us are in that corridor and something goes wrong, Belobog loses the two people who understand the first Stellaron's management system most completely." He pauses. "I'm going because someone who knows what Fragmentum combat looks like needs to be in that group. You're staying because someone who knows what the Stellaron sounds like needs to be reachable above ground." He holds her gaze. "Both of those things are true." She looks at him for a long moment. Then she reaches up and puts her hand on the side of his face — not the stairwell gesture, not the deliberate placement of that day, something faster and less managed, the gesture of a person who is worried and is expressing it in the one way currently available. Her thumb moves once across his cheek. He covers her hand with his and holds it there. "Come back," she says. "Yes," he says. Not I will, not of course — just yes, which is the same as a promise in the specific language they have been developing between them, the one where accuracy is the only currency. She pulls her hand back. She straightens. "Full hourly reports," she says, in the Commander's voice. "Yes," he says again. She turns back to the map, and he goes. —— The entrance to nine-beta at 0700 has a different quality from any previous entry. Luka feels it when he arrives — not the ambient pull of the second heartbeat, which is constant and has been constant, but something layered over it. A heightening. The way a temperature drop feels different when you've been warned about it versus when it arrives without warning — the same cold, the same physics, but with an additional weight of awareness that changes the experience. The others arrive in sequence. Gepard first — armor fully charged, formal configuration, the General's bearing without the General's title. Sampo next, wearing the outer district cold-gear that is his version of preparation, with one of Svarog's extended-range sensor units in a chest harness. Aventurine last, which Luka expected — Aventurine's relationship with punctuality appears to be that he arrives when the moment is right rather than when the clock says so, and in this case the moment is approximately ninety seconds after everyone else, which is enough time to read the group's assembled energy without being early enough to be read himself. Seele is already there when Luka arrives. She has been there long enough to have the sensor calibrated and the frequency recorder from Svarog's storage cases running its baseline reading. She is checking the straps on the recorder's harness — a chest mount, designed to keep the device within her body's proximity radius at all times — and she does not look up when he arrives, which is not rudeness so much as the focused pre-mission quality of someone who has already shifted into the mode that enters dangerous corridors. "The recorder is running," she says, to the strap she is adjusting. "Good," he says. "What's it reading?" "Nothing yet. Svarog said the useful range doesn't start until junction five." She finally looks up. Her eyes move to the equipment case he is carrying, then to his face. "You brought the combat braces." He had, in fact, brought the combat braces — a piece of reinforcing equipment for his prosthetic arm's impact resistance that Svarog had built for tournament use, which significantly improves the structural integrity of the arm under heavy load. "Yes," he says. She looks at them. She looks at his face. "Good," she says, which is the word she uses when she has assessed something and found it correct, and which carries the weight of an acknowledgment without becoming a compliment. Gepard runs the pre-entry briefing in the way he runs everything — efficiently, without wasting words. Junction eight is the boundary between the known and the unknown section. Past eight, the corridor becomes carved bedrock. The lighting drops to torchlight only. The Chrysalis form from Tuesday was suppressed at junction ten but the source corridor is junction twelve, which means eleven is the last safe stopping point. "If the readings change significantly," he says, looking around the group, "we stop and reassess. We are not going to junction twelve without a clear picture of what we're approaching." Something passes between them — not a word, just the acknowledgment of two people who have been in the same district their whole lives and are about to walk into the place that the district is built on top of.
