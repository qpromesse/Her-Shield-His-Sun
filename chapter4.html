Chapter 4: "Load-Bearing"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text The second observation post on the North ridge is completed in four days, which is three days faster than the requisition timeline projected, because Gepard supervised the construction personally and because the soldiers involved understood, from the particular quality of their Commander's presence, that this was not a project with room for delays. It is a small building. Concrete, reinforced, two windows facing north and west respectively, a cable spool array on the ceiling for armor charging, a desk, two chairs, a communication terminal hardwired directly to the Sector A main post rather than running through the palace's general network. This last detail Gepard added himself, without filing a separate requisition for it, on the grounds that a hardwired terminal is structurally indistinguishable from a standard installation and does not require anyone to ask why the new observation post needs a communication line that bypasses the palace switchboard. Bronya will understand why when she sees it. He doesn't examine this thought too closely. —— The tunnel report from Svarog arrives via Sampo, delivered in person to Gepard's quarters at the Restricted Zone's main barracks at seven in the morning, three days after the war council. Sampo hands it over with none of his usual performance — just the folder, a brief summary of the corridor data, and the observation that the crystallization rate has increased by approximately eleven percent since Svarog first recorded it. "Eleven percent in six days," Gepard says. "In the corridors Svarog has sensors in. Which are not all of them." Sampo puts his hands in his pockets. "There's a boy down there — Svarog's apprentice — who knows the outer tunnels better than the sensor maps. I've asked him to do some walkthroughs. He hasn't said yes yet." "But he will." Sampo tips his head. "He will." Gepard looks at the corridor data. Seven-alpha. Nine-beta. The dates beside each entry, and the growth measurements, and the direction — always the same direction, always toward the inhabited sectors, patient and incremental and deeply unconcerned with the walls between it and where it is going. "This goes to Pela today," he says. "I assumed." Sampo turns to leave, and then stops, which is unlike him — Sampo's exits are usually as deliberate as his entrances. "Gepard." He doesn't turn around. "The Supreme Guardian. How is she." Gepard looks at the report. "Working." "That's not what I asked." A pause. "She's managing," Gepard says. "Hm." And Sampo leaves, which means he understood exactly what managing means in this context and has chosen to be tactful about it, which is its own kind of alarming. —— Pela's response to the tunnel data arrives within the hour. It is four pages long and written in Pela's characteristic style, which is to say it is meticulously organized, precisely worded, and absolutely devastating in the manner of a document that has been carefully constructed to be accurate without being alarmist while containing information that is inherently both. The summary is this: the tunnel crystallization, combined with the surface bloom orientation data, constitutes a coherent pattern. The Fragmentum is not spreading randomly. It is converging. And based on the rate of acceleration Pela has calculated from the available data points, the outer perimeter of the Restricted Zone will reach critical density in somewhere between eighteen and twenty-three days. At critical density, standard suppression formations will not be sufficient to contain a bloom. Gepard reads this three times. Then he takes the report, puts on his coat, and walks to the palace. —— He finds Bronya in the administrative wing, not the throne room — she has been conducting most of her Guardian business from a working office on the second floor rather than the throne room, which Gepard suspects is a deliberate choice, the throne room being a space that asks something of the person sitting in it that an office does not. The door is open when he arrives, which means either she is expecting him or she has simply stopped closing doors, and he knocks on the frame anyway. She looks up from her desk. There are three open folders in front of her, two devices running simultaneously, and a cup of something that is probably tea and is definitely cold. She has been here for a while. "You've seen Pela's update," she says. It is not a question. "Yes." He closes the door behind him and crosses to the desk, setting the report down in the available space, which is a smaller area than it should be. She looks at it with the expression of someone confirming something they already knew was there. "Eighteen to twenty-three days." "Seventeen to twenty-two, by my calculation." She straightens a corner of the report without picking it up. "Pela is being conservative." "She usually is." "Yes." A pause. "It's one of her best qualities." He pulls the second chair from the corner of the room and sits down across from her without asking, which he would not have done a month ago, and which she does not comment on now. She is looking at the report but not reading it — her eyes have the unfocused quality of someone reviewing something internally, a calculation already running. "You're going to tell me you've already started drafting a containment strategy," she says. "I've started drafting a containment strategy." "Of course you have." She picks up the cold tea, looks at it, puts it back down. "The standard suppression formations won't hold a critical-density bloom. We both know that." "They won't hold it alone," he says. "With the new observation post on the ridge and proper advance warning, we can mobilize a full response before it reaches critical. Hit it at the peak of the growth curve instead of waiting for it to converge." She looks at him. "That requires positioning soldiers at the observation post on a permanent rotation. In range of whatever the bloom produces when it's pushed back hard." "Yes." "That's dangerous." "It's less dangerous than the alternative." She is quiet for a moment, her eyes on the desk between them. He watches her think — really watches, the way he permits himself to only in private, the way her focus organizes itself into something almost visible, every part of her pointing at the same problem from every angle simultaneously. He has been watching her think since they were seventeen years old, since the first time he saw her standing at the front of a tactical briefing room with a pointer and a map and the absolute conviction of someone who had already solved the problem and was now simply explaining the solution to everyone else. She was seventeen. He had found it somewhat intimidating. He still does. "I want the rotation volunteers only," she says. "No assignments." "Understood." "And I want emergency extraction protocols in place before the first rotation begins." "Already drafted." She looks at him. Something crosses her face — not the almost-smile, something more complicated than that, the expression she gets when she is grateful for something and finds gratitude difficult, which is most of the time. It lasts about two seconds. "Is there anything you haven't already done." "I haven't told you yet." A pause. And there it is — the almost-smile, brief and reluctant and completely genuine. "No," she says. "You haven't." He picks up the cold tea from her desk and stands. "I'll get you a fresh one." "You don't have to—" "You've been here since before six. I passed the kitchen on the way up." He heads for the door. "The containment draft will be on your desk by this afternoon." "Gepard." He stops. "The Stellaron." She says it to the desk, not to him, which is how she says things that cost her something to say. "The broadcasting. I think I know the mechanism." A pause. "It's not transmitting a signal the Fragmentum is following. It's more like—" She stops. Starts again. "You know how geomarrow responds to proximity. How it resonates with other geomarrow sources." "Yes." "The Stellaron is doing something similar. But instead of resonating with other stable sources, it's resonating with the instability in the Fragmentum. Calling to the corrupted matter specifically." She finally looks at him. Her eyes are steady in a way that is clearly costing her something. "Which means the more Fragmentum there is in proximity, the stronger the signal becomes. Which means the more it draws." He stands in the doorway and holds this. "It's a feedback loop," he says. "Yes." "And the Stellaron has been active since—" "Since it was used on Everwinter Hill." She looks back at the desk. "I think the event activated something that was dormant. And I think it's been broadcasting at increasing strength ever since." A pause. "I think it's been getting louder." The hallway behind him is quiet. Somewhere in the administrative wing a door closes, the sound of it traveling through the stone walls and arriving diminished. He looks at her — at the line of her shoulders, at the way her hands are folded on the desk in the fortress position, at the cold tea sitting exactly where he's about to take it — and he thinks about what it costs to know something like this for weeks and carry it alone, waiting to be certain enough to say it out loud. "Why didn't you tell me sooner," he says. Not an accusation. A genuine question. She is quiet for a moment. "Because saying it out loud makes it real. And I needed it to be a theory first." A pause. "I needed time to find out if there was a way to stop it before I had to tell anyone it was happening." "And is there." "I don't know yet." She picks up a pen and sets it down again. "Pela is working on it. There are some Stellaron research records from before the Separation — fragmentary, incomplete — that reference resonance dampening. The theory is that if you can interrupt the frequency, you can break the feedback loop." She looks at him. "But the only thing powerful enough to generate an interrupting frequency at that scale is another geomarrow source of comparable magnitude." He looks at her. "There isn't one," she says, before he can ask. "Not that we know of. Not anymore." A pause. "But I'm looking." He takes the cold tea. He stands in the doorway for a moment longer. "You should have told me sooner," he says. She doesn't answer, which is its own answer. "Bronya." "I know," she says. "I know." He goes to get her a fresh cup of tea, and he does not say anything else, because there is nothing else to say that the silence isn't already saying more accurately. —— He is halfway down the administrative wing corridor when he nearly walks into Seele. She is coming from the opposite direction, moving fast, carrying a folder and wearing the expression of someone who has been in three back-to-back meetings and is on her way to a fourth and is managing this with approximately the grace you would expect from someone who spent most of her life in the Underworld and considers bureaucratic scheduling a personal affront. She pulls up short when she sees him. He pulls up short when he sees her. They stand in the corridor for a moment. "Commander Gepard," she says. "Seele." He nods. "She's in the office. The door's open." Seele looks at him, then at the cup of cold tea in his hand, then back at him, with the specific expressiveness of someone who has registered several things simultaneously and chosen to process them in order of priority. "Is she eating." "I don't know." "She never eats when she's working." She shifts the folder under her arm. "She did the same thing when she was fourteen, before any kind of assessment or review. Serval used to make her eat something first or she'd work herself into a headache by noon." She seems to realize she's said this out loud and a complicated expression crosses her face — the expression of someone briefly forgetting where they are in the timeline, stepping accidentally into a warmer and more distant version of things before the intervening years made everything complicated. She straightens. "I'll bring something up." "I'd appreciate it," he says. She gives him a look that is not unfriendly, exactly, but is the look of someone taking a measurement. "You were at the convergence site the other night." "Yes." "How bad is it going to get." He considers giving her the careful version. He looks at her and decides against it — Seele is not someone who responds well to the careful version, and more relevantly, she is someone who will find out the accurate version regardless and will simply think less of you for not giving it to her the first time. "Bad," he says. "We have a window. We're going to use it." She holds his gaze for a moment, doing the same calculation he did on her — deciding how much weight to give the answer, deciding whether it's an honest one. Whatever she concludes, she nods. Once, sharp, the accepting nod. "Right." She moves past him toward the office. He continues toward the kitchen. He is three steps from the kitchen door when he hears voices inside — one of which he recognizes as belonging to two of the administrative aides who have been working the palace morning shift, and the other of which he does not recognize at first, and then does. He stops outside the kitchen door. "—told him three times the delivery was wrong," the voice is saying, with the cheerful aggression of someone retelling a story they clearly enjoyed living. "Three times. And each time he checked his manifest and said no, this is correct, these are the right components. And I said, with respect, these are generator coupling flanges, I ordered drive shaft couplings, they're not the same thing, and he said—" Laughter from the aides. "—he said, and I quote, a coupling is a coupling—" More laughter. Gepard pushes the kitchen door open. Luka is sitting on the counter — on the counter, in the palace kitchen — with a mug of something, talking to two palace administrative aides who are supposed to be on shift and are currently wiping tears of laughter from their eyes. He stops when he sees Gepard in the doorway, and his expression does a rapid calculation. "Commander Gepard," he says, with the particular composure of someone who knows they're probably not supposed to be somewhere and has decided that confidence is the best available strategy. "How did you get into the palace," Gepard says. "Sampo has a contact at the service entrance." He says this without any apparent guilt. "I have a delivery. Generator components for the Sector B charge stations." He holds up a bill of lading, which is, in fact, legitimate. Gepard looks at it. The components are listed correctly. The authorization is in order. "I was waiting for someone to sign off." Gepard looks at the two aides, who are now studiously engaged with their own mugs. He looks at Luka. He takes the bill of lading, reviews it for precisely the amount of time it requires, and signs it. "The Sector B receiving bay is through the east corridor and down one level," he says. "Right." Luka hops off the counter without self-consciousness. "Thanks." He retrieves his bill of lading, moves toward the door, and pauses. Something in his face shifts — a brief evaluation, the same quality of assessment Gepard had seen in Seele's face in the corridor, except younger and less practiced. "The tunnels," he says. "I told Sampo yes. About the walkthroughs." Gepard looks at him. "Figured you should know," Luka says. "Since it's your operation." "It's not—" He stops. In any meaningful practical sense, it is his operation, and the boy apparently knows it. "Thank you." Luka nods, easy and unhurried, and goes. Gepard stands in the kitchen for a moment. He fills a fresh mug from the pot on the counter, finds a plate, and after a brief inspection of the available provisions, constructs something that will function as breakfast by any reasonable standard. He puts it on a tray. He goes back upstairs. —— Seele is in the office when he returns, sitting in the chair he'd pulled from the corner, talking to Bronya in the low, slightly careful way of two people who are still learning the updated version of each other. The conversation pauses when he comes in. He sets the tray on the desk without comment and takes up a position near the window with his own mug, which is his way of saying I am not interrupting, and also I am not leaving. Bronya looks at the tray. She looks at him. She picks up the mug. "You didn't have to," she says. "You said that last time too." Seele looks between them with the alert, evaluative attention of someone calibrating a room. She says nothing about it, which Gepard appreciates. "The district administrators in the second and third sectors have agreed to the alert capacity increase," Seele says, returning to what she was saying. "The first sector is going to be a problem. Administrator Venn has been—" She pauses, selecting a word. "Resistant." "Venn has been resistant to every change of policy since the Separation," Bronya says, with the precise, contained weariness of someone who has encountered this wall before and is measuring it. "He was resistant under Cocolia's administration too. He is not political resistance, he is institutional inertia." She picks up something from her desk — not the Pela report, a different document. "I'll speak with him directly." "He'll respond better to you than to a directive," Seele agrees. Then, after a pause: "He'll also take longer." "I know." She sets the document down. "We don't have longer, so I'll have to do it faster than I'd like." She looks at Gepard. "How quickly can you get the observation post rotation operational." "First rotation starts tomorrow night." Seele looks at him. "You already have volunteers." "Chen and Mira, from the Sector A night squad. Two others from Sector B." He pauses. "And Rikov." "The new one?" Bronya says. "He asked." He says this in a tone that leaves no room for further discussion, because Rikov asking matters, and turning down a volunteer in front of his entire unit has consequences for morale that outweigh the comfort of keeping a new soldier away from the sharp end. "He's been briefed fully on the risk." Bronya looks at him for a moment, then nods. "Good," she says. "Then we have seventeen days, at Pela's most conservative estimate." She looks at the map on the wall of the office — a large-format version of the one in the council chamber, the bloom orientation data overlaid in amber. "I want a full council review in three days. By then I want the dampening research to have something usable, the tunnel walkthroughs started, and the outer perimeter reinforced to the spec in Gepard's containment draft." She pauses. "Questions." "One," Seele says. "What do we tell people." The room is quiet for a moment. "The truth," Bronya says. "As much of it as we have, stated as clearly as we can, without speculation." She picks up the mug. "People in this city have been lied to by their government for long enough. I will not do that." A pause. "But I will also not tell them things I am not certain of. Not yet." Seele looks at her. Her expression does something complicated — something that has history in it, something that predates the last few months, that goes back further than Belobog's opening and the Trailblazers and everything that came after. Something that is very simply: I know you. And underneath it, quieter: I'm glad you're here. She doesn't say any of this. She just nods. "Alright." —— The first thing that goes wrong happens on day three. The North ridge observation post is staffed by Chen and one of the Sector B volunteers, a woman named Hessa who has eight years in the Restricted Zone and the particular calm that produces. They are running a standard monitoring shift, documenting bloom behavior, sending hourly reports through the hardwired terminal, doing everything correctly. At 3:14 AM, the Fragmentum blooms. Not at the North ridge convergence site. At the East wall of the Restricted Zone, Sector C, a site with no prior convergence history, no preliminary amber crystallization, no warning indicators of any kind. It simply erupts — a full bloom, dense and fast, the fastest growth rate Gepard has ever seen in eight years of Restricted Zone service — and it does so in the space between two patrol rotations, in the twenty-minute window where that section of the perimeter is, technically, lightly covered. The alarm comes through at 3:17 AM. Gepard is at the Sector A main post when it hits, reviewing the night's reports. He has his coat on and is moving before the alarm finishes its first cycle, because this is simply what his body does when it hears that sound — it has been doing it for eight years and will probably keep doing it until it no longer can. He sends the mobilization order to all three sectors simultaneously while he runs. It takes eleven minutes to reach Sector C. By the time he gets there, the bloom is thirty meters wide and growing. By the time the full response formation reaches the site, it is forty-five. They push. They push for three hours, and this time it is harder than the North ridge — harder in the specific way that comes from fighting something that is moving differently than it has before, with a speed and a density that does not respond to the same suppression patterns, that redirects faster, that seems — and he hates this word, he hates how much he hates this word — that seems to learn. He fires the same pattern twice at the same formation and the second time it redirects before the lance connects, and the cold in the back of his mind where he keeps the things he doesn't have time to process right now gets a little colder. At the four-hour mark, the bloom is still at forty meters. At the four-hour mark, Rikov takes a Fragmentum impact to his left shoulder. It is not catastrophic. The geomarrow pauldron absorbs the primary force; the crystallization is contained to the surface of the armor and a thin line of his upper arm where the pauldron's edge didn't cover. Standard exposure injury, the kind Gepard has seen dozens of times, the kind Natasha can treat with her eyes closed. It will heal cleanly. But Rikov goes down, and in the second it takes for the soldier beside him to drag him back behind the shield line, the bloom's southern edge advances four meters. "Pull the line back to the secondary perimeter," Gepard says, and his voice is completely level, because his voice being level is not optional. "Yuna, take the south flank. Everyone else — hold the secondary line. We are not letting this move further." They hold the secondary line. At the sixth hour, with the sky going grey with coming dawn, the bloom finally, grudgingly, begins to contract. At the seventh hour, it collapses. Gepard stands in the middle of Sector C in the silence after, his charge at eleven percent, his right arm aching from six hours of sustained geomarrow discharge, and he looks at the damage. The bloom has scorched a forty-meter radius of the Restricted Zone's eastern perimeter. Two sections of the perimeter wall are compromised — not breached, but stressed, the structural integrity flagged on his display. The ground within the bloom radius is saturated with residual Fragmentum crystallization that will need to be cleared before it can influence another convergence. And it is pointing north-northwest. Toward the inhabited districts. Closer than the North ridge convergence had been. He looks at the residual pattern for a long moment in the cold quiet of the early morning, and then he takes out his phone and photographs it, and then he calls Pela. "I saw the alarm," Pela says, before he speaks. "I've been up since 3. What's the damage." He gives her the numbers. He hears her write them down. When he finishes there is a pause that has a specific quality to it — the quality of a person processing something they expected and are not made more comfortable by the confirmation of. "The site had no prior convergence history," she says. "None." "The bloom emerged in a patrol gap." "A twenty-minute window." Another pause. "Gepard. That's not random." "No," he says. "It's not." He hangs up and calls the palace. The call connects after two rings, which means she was awake, which means either she heard the alarm or she never went to sleep. "Tell me," Bronya says. He tells her. All of it — the patrol gap, the growth rate, the redirection behavior, the residual pattern orientation, Rikov's injury, the compromised wall sections. He gives her the numbers exactly as he gave them to Pela, because she deserves the exact numbers, and because giving her anything less than the exact numbers is a form of lying he will not do. She is quiet for a moment after he finishes. "The patrol gap," she says. "The Fragmentum knew where it was." "It behaved as though it did." "How." "I don't know yet." He looks at the scorched ground. "But it's consistent with what Pela has been calling behavioral clustering. It's not just orienting toward population density. It's orienting away from resistance." "It's mapping us," she says. Not a question. "That's what it looks like." Another silence. Longer this time. He can hear her breathing, just barely, on the other end of the line — the controlled, steady breath of someone keeping very still while they think. He waits. "Randomize all patrol schedules effective immediately," she says. "No pattern longer than forty-eight hours. I want the rotations generated by Pela's office, not by standard military scheduling, so there's no algorithmic predictability to them." A pause. "And I want the wall sections assessed by end of today." "Already requested the structural team." "Of course you have." Her voice, for a moment, has something in it that is not quite the Commander register — something more tired, and more human, and more familiar. "Is Rikov alright." "He'll be fine. Natasha will see him this morning." "Good." A pause. "Gepard. This is going to get worse before it gets better." "I know." "I need you to tell me honestly — do we have what we need to hold the perimeter for the next two weeks while Pela finds the dampening solution." He looks at the bloom site. He looks at the scorched ground and the stressed wall sections and the residual crystallization pointing north-northwest with that patient, enormous, completely indifferent orientation. He thinks about six hours of pushing against something that learned faster than they could adapt. He thinks about what critical density means when it arrives, and the formation strength they currently have, and the seventeen days Pela gave them at the conservative estimate, which is now fourteen. He thinks about what the honest answer is. "We can hold it," he says. "But not without cost." A silence. "Alright," she says. And then: "Come to the palace when you're done with the site assessment. I'll be in the office." "I'll be there by noon." "I'll have something to eat," she says, which is as close as she gets right now to admitting that Seele told her she needs to eat, and he does not point this out. "You will also eat." "Understood, Commander." She hangs up. He puts his phone away. He looks at the eastern perimeter, at the grey dawn coming in over the walls of the Restricted Zone, at the amber light of the inhabited districts that can just barely be seen from here on a clear morning if you know where to look. He knows where to look. He turns back to his soldiers. "Alright," he says. "Let's get to work." —— Notes: The bloom emerged in the patrol gap. It KNEW where the patrol gap was.
