Chapter 11: "Outside Looking In"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text The letter arrives on a Wednesday. It comes through official diplomatic channels, which means it arrives first on Pela's desk, which means Pela reads it before anyone else does, which means when she brings it to Bronya's office at 0900 her expression is already the carefully calibrated one — the one that is not alarmed and is not performing the absence of alarm, but is simply, precisely, holding something at measured distance while she decides what to do with it. Bronya reads it. She reads it twice. She sets it on the desk with the particular care of someone setting down something that has the potential to be heavy. "The IPC," she says. "The Interastral Peace Corporation," Pela confirms, which is not a clarification Bronya needs and which Pela offers anyway, because naming things precisely is how Pela processes them. "The letter is from their External Relations division. It's a formal inquiry — not a demand, not a directive. An inquiry." She pauses. "They've been monitoring Belobog's energy output signatures for some time. The Stellaron event at Everwinter Hill generated readings they have apparently been trying to account for for several months. The recent disruption of the broadcast signal—" She pauses again. "The array. They detected it." "From outside the planet," Bronya says. "Their monitoring infrastructure is extensive," Pela says, which is the diplomatic version of yes, from outside the planet, and also somewhat further. Bronya looks at the letter. She does not pick it up again. "What do they want." "They want to send a representative. A preliminary assessment visit, they call it — to verify the stability of the Stellaron and evaluate Belobog's capacity for ongoing management." A pause. "The language is very careful. Very courteous." Another pause. "Courteous language from the IPC typically means they have already decided what they intend to do and are giving you the opportunity to agree before they do it without you." Bronya looks at her. "How long have you known about the IPC's monitoring." "I suspected it after the Everwinter Hill readings. I confirmed it after the array." Pela meets her gaze steadily. "I should have said something sooner. I was waiting until I had more complete information." A silence. "I understand," Bronya says, which is not forgiveness and is not condemnation — it is simply the accurate response of someone who has been doing the same thing for two months and knows exactly what waiting for more complete information costs and why people do it anyway. "When do they want to send the representative." "The letter requests a response within two weeks. The visit itself—" Pela looks at the letter. "They suggest one month from the date of our response." "Six weeks," Bronya says. "Approximately." Another silence. Bronya stands, which is the thing she does when she is thinking at a scale that the chair cannot contain — she moves to the window, the south-facing window of the administrative wing, and looks at the city below it. It is a clear day for Belobog — the blizzard has pulled back to a light snowfall, and the inhabited districts are visible from here, the amber warmth of them going about their ordinary business in the ordinary morning. Pela waits. "Tell Gepard," Bronya says, to the window. "I have a message queued—" "Tell him in person." She turns. "I want him in this room today, not by message." She looks at Pela. "Please." Pela nods, and goes. —— He reads the letter standing up, which is how he reads things that require immediate and total attention. He reads it once, and then he reads the specific paragraph about the assessment visit a second time, and then he looks at Pela over the paper. "Six weeks," he says. "Approximately." "What do they actually want." "To assess Belobog's capacity for ongoing Stellaron management," Pela says, and the flatness in her voice is the specific flatness of someone quoting language they do not trust. "Which means they want to know whether to treat Belobog as a sovereign entity capable of managing its own existential risk, or as a situation that requires external — management." He holds the letter. He thinks about the IPC. He knows what most people in Belobog know, which is not very much — a name, a reach, a vague institutional weight that exists mostly in the context of trade agreements and occasional mentions in old correspondence. He knows, because he has spent eight years protecting a city that has been functionally isolated from the rest of the universe, that the scope of what he does not know about the IPC is considerably larger than what he does. He thinks about Bronya. He thinks about what she has built in three months — the council, the protocols, the fractured resonance solution, the wall that held, the Stellaron sitting at 1.3 and flat. He thinks about her at the window this morning, the loose hair, the open hands, the city she has been holding up with the same arms that trembled in the array room. He thinks about an external organization deciding whether those efforts constitute sufficient management. "I'll be at the office in twenty minutes," he says. —— She is at the window when he arrives, which is where she was when he arrived this morning, except this time she is in full armor and her hair is up and her hands are behind her back, which is the difference between the woman at the window in the morning and the Guardian at the window in the afternoon, and he has learned to see both. He closes the door. He does not always close the door, but today he closes it. She turns. "You've read it," she says. "Yes." "Pela gave you her assessment." "Yes." He crosses to the desk and sets the letter down. "What's your read." She looks at the letter. "The IPC does not send courtesy visits. They send evaluations. The language is careful because they want compliance before confrontation — they would rather we welcome them than have to require it." She pauses. "Which means they have leverage they are not yet showing." Another pause. "The question is what they intend to do with it." "Absorb us," he says flatly. She looks at him. "That's what corporations do with assets they consider undertended," he says. "I don't know the IPC's specific mechanisms. But the logic is the same." He meets her gaze. "They've been monitoring the planet for months. They detected the broadcast signal escalation. They detected the disruption. They know we have a Stellaron and they know we've been managing a crisis with it and they want to know whether we're going to keep being capable of that or whether they need to step in." He pauses. "And stepping in has a different meaning when you're the IPC." She is quiet for a moment. "Yes," she says. "That's my read too." She moves from the window to the desk and stands beside it, not behind it — beside it, which puts her a meter from him instead of two, which is a thing she has been doing more frequently since the stairwell, and which he has been noticing with the same discipline he applies to everything he is noticing and not examining. "Six weeks," she says. "What can we do in six weeks." "Document everything," he says immediately, because he has been thinking about this since Pela's office. "The full record of the past three months. The Fragmentum escalation, the behavioral clustering, the feedback loop mechanism, the fractured resonance solution, the array construction. Everything. With Pela's analysis, Serval's calibration data, the structural team's certifications." He pauses. "We show them a body of work that makes the case that Belobog managed this, that Belobog has the people and the methodology and the infrastructure to continue managing it, that external intervention is not only unnecessary but would be actively disruptive to a system that is currently working." She is looking at him the way she looks at him when he is giving her the thing she needed before she knew she needed it. "That's going to take significant time to compile." "Pela can organize it. I'll provide the military records. Serval can provide the technical documentation before she leaves Thursday." He pauses. "You write the summary. The overall account — what happened, how it was addressed, what the current status is." He looks at her. "In your voice. Not a bureaucratic summary. Your voice." She holds his gaze. "Why." "Because whoever the IPC sends is going to be looking for reasons to find this insufficient. A bureaucratic document gives them surfaces to critique. Your account of it—" He pauses, choosing the word. "Doesn't." She is quiet for a moment. Something in her expression moves — the thing it does when he says something she did not expect to agree with as completely as she does. "You have a lot of faith in my voice," she says. "I've been listening to it for five years," he says. "It's not faith." She looks at him. He looks at her. The letter is on the desk between them and the closed door is behind him and the city is below the south window and the stairwell is three weeks ago and also entirely present, the way certain mornings are. She says, very quietly: "Gepard." "Yes." "This morning." She is looking at him with the specific directness she has been deploying since the stairwell — the one that does not deflect, that stays on the thing it is looking at. "In the stairwell." She pauses. "I've been thinking about it." "So have I," he says, which is the understatement of the month and they both know it. "I don't want to—" She stops. Starts again. "I don't want it to be a thing that happened and is now filed somewhere. I'm—" She looks at the desk briefly, and then back at him. "I'm not good at this," she says, which is the most disarmed thing she has said to him that isn't at 2 AM. "I'm not good at — saying the thing directly. In the daylight. Without the dark or the 2 AM to make it easier." "You're doing it right now," he says. "I know. It's very difficult." The corner of her mouth. The genuine one. "You're not helping." "I'm not trying to make it easier," he says. "I want to hear what you're actually saying." She looks at him for a moment. Then: "I don't want to step back from it." A pause. "From — this. Whatever this is." She gestures, slightly, between them, which is the most uncoordinated gesture he has ever seen her make and which lands in his chest like a fist. "I know the timing is—" "The timing is what it is," he says. "The IPC—" "Is six weeks away." He looks at her. "That's six weeks from now. This is now." She holds his gaze for a long moment. The afternoon light from the south window has moved across the room since he arrived, and it sits now on the edge of the desk between them, and outside the city does its ordinary beautiful thing of being alive and continuing. She takes a step. Not the same step as the stairwell — not the slow, measured, deliberate one. A smaller step, the natural closing of a distance that has been getting smaller anyway, until she is standing close enough that he is aware of her armor's warmth and the specific scent of cold air that lives in geomarrow-charged metal and that he associates, at a level below conscious thought, with her specifically. She tilts her head back to look at him. He looks down at her. The geometry of it — the same as the stairwell, both of them choosing it. She reaches up and rests her hand against his chest, over the armor, and looks at him. It is not the jaw. It is not the precise, deliberate placement of the stairwell. It is something quieter than that — a hand over armor, over the geomarrow charge and the breastplate and everything beneath it, and she is looking up at him with no composure requirement and no management and nothing held back. "I noticed you when I was seventeen," she says. "I have been noticing you every day since. And I have spent a very long time deciding whether to do anything about it." She pauses. "I've decided." He covers her hand with his, the same way he did in the stairwell. His thumb moves across her knuckles — deliberate this time, not involuntary — and she watches his hand on hers and then looks back up at him. "Bronya," he says, and she looks at him, and he says: "I know." She blinks. "That's all you're going to say." "I've been waiting for you to decide for five years," he says. "I know." Something happens on her face that is not the almost-smile and not the genuine one but is something she does not usually show — something that is simply, entirely, happy. Brief. Real. Gone before it fully surfaces but present enough to see. "You're very annoying," she says, which is not what she means, and they both know it. "I know," he says, which is not what he means either, and they both know that too. He leans down — slowly, with the same deliberateness as everything between them, nothing rushed, nothing taken — and presses his forehead to hers. She stills. He stills. They stand like that, foreheads together, her hand on his chest and his hand over hers, the armor warm between them and the afternoon light doing what it does and the letter on the desk and the IPC six weeks away and the world outside exactly as large and complicated as it has always been. For now, for this moment, it is also exactly this small. "The documentation," she says, after a while. "Yes," he says. "We should start today." "We should." "Pela is going to need the military records by Thursday if Serval's technical files are going where I think they're going." "I'll have them ready tonight." She pulls back, slightly — not away, just back, to a distance where they can see each other properly. She looks at him. Her hand is still on his chest. His is still over hers. "Okay," she says. "Okay," he says. She steps back. He lets her. She turns to the desk and picks up the IPC letter, and he watches the Guardian's posture settle back into place — the hands behind her back, the precise attention — except that it settles differently now, the same way it settled differently after the array. A little less sealed. A little more chosen. "Sit down," she says, in the Commander's voice, except it has a warmth underneath it now that he can hear from two meters away and that she is not bothering to suppress. "We have six weeks and a significant amount of work to do." He sits down. He picks up the patrol folder. He is, with a certainty that has nothing to do with Fragmentum or walls or broadcast frequencies, going to be completely useless at reading this patrol folder for at least twenty minutes. He reads it anyway. —— That evening, Serval finds him in the corridor outside the construction chamber doing a final charge check on his armor, and she says: "Okay, I'm saying it now." He looks at her. "I said I'd say it at a worse time," she says. "But actually I think now is probably fine because I also want to say congratulations and it's hard to lead with congratulations if I do the other thing first." She crosses her arms. "So. Congratulations." He looks at her for a moment. "How did you know," he says. "You have been smiling at your patrol folder all evening," she says. "Also I ran into Seele and she said Bronya came to the afternoon briefing with her hair down and Gepard was smiling and she said she was 'just noting that.'" Serval pauses. "Also I have known you for your entire life." He looks at the wall. "The thing I was going to say," she says, and her voice changes — drops from the lightness to something underneath it, something that is genuine Serval in the register she reserves for things that matter. "Is that she is — she has been through things. You know what she's been through and you know it better than most, and she is doing well now, and she is stronger than anyone gives her credit for, and she is going to be fine." She pauses. "But she is also — she holds things very tightly because she learned to. And what she needs is someone who will be there when she is ready to put things down, not someone who will try to make her put them down before she's ready." She looks at him. "You know how to do that. You've been doing it for years." A pause. "Don't stop." He looks at his sister. "Serval," he says. "I know," she says. "Don't make it a thing." She unfolds her arms and steps forward and hugs him, which she has done his entire life and which he permits, specifically, because it is her. He puts one arm around her shoulders. She squeezes once and pulls back. "The technical files will be organized and ready for Pela by tomorrow morning," she says, returning to normal register as if she was never in the other one. "The calibration data, the frequency documentation, the array specifications — all of it. In a format that will make the IPC's representative feel like they are reviewing the work of a research institution, not a city that has been improvising." She pauses. "Because it was good work. We did good work here." "We did," he says. "And Gepard." She is already moving toward the stairwell. "The patrol folder. You should probably actually read it." He looks at her. She grins — unguarded, Serval-genuine — and goes. He looks at the patrol folder. He reads it, this time, all the way through. —— Three days later, Serval leaves on the Thursday morning train, and the palace is slightly quieter without her, in the way that all spaces are slightly quieter in the aftermath of someone with significant energy. Gepard sees her off at the platform, which is the kind of thing brothers do and which he would feel embarrassed about if Serval did not also take approximately six minutes to check that he is warm enough and has eaten and has been sleeping adequately, so they are square on the embarrassment front. "I'll come back," she says. "I know." "When things are—" She looks at him. "Well, you'll message me." "When things are interesting," he says, which is the code they have always used, since they were children in the Landau house with the cold corridors and the weight of expectations and the private vocabulary they developed to avoid saying the serious things directly. "When things are interesting," she agrees. She squeezes his arm once. She gets on the train. He watches it go. He stands on the platform in the early morning cold with the Belobog platform noise around him — the sounds of ordinary transit, ordinary movement, people going from one place to another in the ordinary way — and he thinks about the IPC's letter on Bronya's desk and the six weeks ahead and the documentation project that Pela has already organized into a structure that would make a research institution envious. He thinks about the stairwell and the afternoon office and the foreheads together and the armor warm between them and okay said twice, quietly, in the afternoon light. He thinks about the weight of everything that is coming. He thinks about the specific quality of the morning — the light arriving top-down, the city below the platform still half-dark, the spires of the palace catching the first of the dawn. He thinks: worth protecting. He has always thought this. He will keep thinking it. He goes back to work. —— In the Underworld, four days after the nine-beta junction conversation, Luka is in Svarog's workshop when Sampo arrives. Sampo looks at him. He looks at Sampo. Something in Sampo's expression is different from usual — less performance, more the stripped version, the one that showed up at Natasha's ward and in the tunnel report forward and in the council chamber where he brought his notes on a playing card. "The IPC sent a letter," Sampo says. Luka sets down the component he was working on. "What's the IPC." Sampo looks at him for a moment with the expression of someone deciding how much to say and settling on: enough. "An organization," he says. "Very large. Very far-reaching. They monitor Stellarons across — various locations. They've been watching Belobog." He pauses. "They want to send someone. To evaluate." "Evaluate what." "Whether Belobog is managing its own situation adequately." He sits down in the chair — Svarog's chair, the one for human visitors — and leans back with the ease of someone who has been in this workshop many times. "Whether they need to intervene." Luka looks at him. "And if they decide we're not managing adequately." "Then they manage it for us." A pause. "Which is not the same as us managing it." The workshop is quiet for a moment. Svarog, at the back, works without comment, which is his version of listening. "Is Bronya—" Luka starts. "She knows," Sampo says. "Gepard too. Pela's already building the documentation." He looks at Luka. "Which is why I'm here." He leans forward. "The tunnel logs. Your walkthrough records — all eighteen days of them. The sensor readings, the pulse documentation, the crystallization progression, the reorientation event." He meets Luka's eyes. "That's evidence. That's a record of what was happening down here, documented by someone who was in the tunnels while it was happening. The IPC's representative is going to want the full picture — surface and underground. Your logs are the underground half." Luka looks at him. "You want my logs for the documentation," he says. "I want your logs for the documentation." A pause. "They're already organized," Luka says. "I've been keeping them clean since the first walkthrough." He looks at his hands. "I didn't know why at the time. I just — kept them clean." Something in Sampo's face does the thing it does sometimes — the brief, genuine version, visible for a second before it goes back behind the performance. "Good," he says. "That's good, kid." "I'm twenty-one." "I know." Sampo stands. "Send them to Pela. Tonight if you can." He moves toward the door and does the thing, and Luka is beginning to understand that everyone in this city does the thing, which is stop in the doorway. "Luka," Sampo says. "Yeah." "You're going to meet the IPC representative." He says it like a fact, not a prediction. "When they come. Whoever they send. You're going to be in the room." He looks at Luka with the evaluative eyes — the real ones, not the performance. "Because you know those tunnels and you documented them and that's going to matter." A pause. "Just so you know what's coming." Luka looks at him. "Okay," he says. Sampo nods. He goes. Luka sits in the workshop with the component in his hand and the tunnel logs already organized neatly on his phone, and he thinks about the surface and the palace and the IPC and the six weeks ahead, and he thinks about corridor nine-beta and the way the crystallization is dulling and the junction where he keeps staying too long. "Svarog," he says. "Yes," Svarog says, from the back. "What do you know about the IPC." A pause. The sound of deliberate, unhurried work. "Enough," Svarog says, "to know that what Belobog presents to them in six weeks will matter for a very long time." Another pause. "And enough to know that the people preparing that presentation are capable of making it sufficient." A pause. "If they trust each other." Luka thinks about this. "Do they," he says. "Trust each other." Svarog is quiet for a moment. His optical sensors, visible from where Luka sits, carry the expression they carry when he is about to say something true and simple that will nonetheless take a while to land. "More than they did three months ago," he says. "Which is the direction that matters." Luka nods.
