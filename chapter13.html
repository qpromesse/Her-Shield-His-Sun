Chapter 13: "Two Visitors"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text They arrive on a Tuesday, which Gepard finds faintly offensive. Not for any rational reason. There is nothing wrong with Tuesday as a day. It is simply that Tuesday is the day he runs the full perimeter inspection of all three sectors, which takes four hours and requires his complete attention and cannot be rescheduled without cascading consequences for the patrol rotation, and having two IPC representatives arrive on the same morning as the full perimeter inspection creates a scheduling conflict that is nobody's fault and which he resents anyway. He delegates the final sector of the inspection to Chen — who is capable, thorough, and has been running observation post rotations without complaint for six weeks and has more than earned the responsibility — and arrives at the palace's formal entrance at 0940, which is eleven minutes after the IPC transport landed at Belobog's outer platform and nine minutes before the time Pela specified for the formal reception. He is not late. He takes a moment in the corridor outside the entrance hall to check his armor — charge at ninety-four percent, all seven pieces correctly seated, the formal dress configuration that takes slightly longer to assemble and which he has worn approximately twelve times in his career and which he knows, because Serval told him once with considerable feeling, that he wears well. He does not think about this. He pushes the door open and goes in. —— The first thing he notices about Aventurine is that he is already looking at something he shouldn't be. Not aggressively. Not obviously. The specific quality of attention that belongs to someone who takes in a room without appearing to, who is ostensibly reading the formal welcome documentation that Pela prepared and is actually cataloguing everything within visual range simultaneously, the way a person does when they have been trained — or have trained themselves — to never look directly at what they are actually looking at. Gepard recognizes this immediately, because it is a skill he possesses himself, and because it takes one to know one, and because the thing Aventurine is not-looking-at directly is the doorway Bronya came through, which means he arrived before her, which means he has already been reading the room for however long he has been standing in it without her as the reference point. He is tall, blond, well-dressed in the IPC's particular brand of wealth that manages to look both formal and entirely relaxed, and he has the expression of a man who finds everything slightly entertaining, which Gepard identifies as a professional posture rather than a personality trait within approximately thirty seconds of being in the room with him. Beside him is Topaz. Topaz, Gepard has met once before — briefly, peripherally, during her initial visit to Belobog several months ago. He had not been in the room for the formal meeting, had only passed her in the administrative wing corridor long enough to form the assessment: thorough, precise, moves like someone who already knows the answer to every question she's about to ask. He had been correct. She is looking at Pela's welcome documentation with the attention of someone who has already read the version she compiled from external sources and is now comparing it to the official one, annotating the discrepancies in real time in the back of her mind. She looks up when he enters. "Commander Gepard," she says, which means she knows his name and has placed his face, which means she prepared. "Administrator Topaz," he says, which means he knows her title, which means he also prepared. They understand each other immediately in the way that two prepared people always do. "This is my colleague," Topaz says, with a gesture toward Aventurine that manages to convey affection and professional distance simultaneously. "Aventurine," says Aventurine, extending a hand. His eyes are very light — a pale aquamarine, the specific color of something deep and cold — and they are, Gepard notes, currently aimed directly at him, which means he has shifted from not-looking-directly to looking-directly, which means he made a decision about Gepard fast. "I've heard good things." "Have you," Gepard says, which is not a question. "The documentation your administrator sent was thorough." A pause, the precise length of a pause that is doing something — not quite a smile, not quite not a smile, the expression of a man who has made a decision to be charming and is executing it with the facility of someone who has been doing it for a long time. "Unusually thorough." "Belobog has had an eventful few months," Gepard says. "So I gather." The light eyes move past him, briefly, to the door behind him. Not impatiently — just noting. "We're looking forward to meeting the Supreme Guardian." "She'll be here shortly," Gepard says, and does not say: she is already here, she has been here for twenty minutes, she is standing behind the door to the left of the entrance hall waiting for the moment that is precisely right to enter, because Bronya does not arrive to anything — she makes arrivals, and there is a difference. He does not say this because it is not information that needs to be volunteered, and because the fact that Aventurine is watching the door means he has already suspected it, and Gepard sees no reason to confirm the suspicion early. He moves to stand at the position Pela designated, and waits. —— She enters at 0951. He watches Aventurine's face when she enters, because Aventurine's face is the most informative thing in the room at this moment, and what he sees there is: recalibration. The practiced ease shifts — not disappears, just shifts, the way a person shifts when the room changes shape around them. The light eyes track her from the entrance to the center of the room with the specific attention of someone updating an assessment they had already formed, and whatever the assessment updates to is something he puts back behind the expression very quickly. Topaz's response is subtler and in some ways more alarming — a very slight forward lean, the body language of increased attention, and then a return to stillness that is more deliberate than her previous stillness had been. They both made a calculation when she walked in. Gepard files this. Bronya addresses them both with the precise, warm formality she has been deploying with increasing fluency since she became Guardian — the version of her voice that is both accessible and unarguable, that says: I am in charge of this room and I am also pleased you are in it, and both of those things are true simultaneously. She thanks them for coming. She expresses Belobog's interest in a productive assessment. She uses the word assessment, which is the IPC's word and not her own, and she uses it in a way that acknowledges their framing without conceding to it, and Gepard watches Topaz note this and file it. Aventurine says: "Supreme Guardian. Your city has had quite a year." "It has," Bronya says, with an equanimity that does not invite elaboration. "The Stellaron event at Everwinter Hill. The feedback loop. The fractured resonance solution." He pauses. "The array." He tilts his head slightly. "Remarkable, for a city that has been functionally isolated from the broader network for — how long?" "Long enough," Bronya says, "to know how to manage our own problems." Aventurine looks at her for a moment. The entertained-by-everything expression is present and real and also completely decorative, a surface over something that is doing considerably more work. "Yes," he says, pleasantly. "That comes through clearly in the documentation." "Good," Bronya says. "That was the intent." A beat. Then Aventurine smiles — genuinely, Gepard thinks, or at least with more genuine content than the previous smile — and says: "I think we're going to get along well, Supreme Guardian." "I think," Bronya says, "that we are going to have a productive assessment." Which is not the same thing, and everyone in the room knows it, and Aventurine's expression says that he appreciates the distinction. —— The first day is Topaz's. This is not formally scheduled — the itinerary shows a joint welcome session followed by a general documentation review — but it becomes apparent within the first two hours that Topaz and Aventurine operate on different rhythms, with different methodologies, and the documentation review is primarily Topaz's domain. Aventurine is present, seated at the table, and he asks approximately four questions in three hours, each of which is so precisely targeted that Gepard finds himself checking the documentation to see what the question is actually asking, which is never exactly what the words say. Topaz moves through Pela's documentation with the systematic focus of a machine doing work it was built for — not dismissive, not aggressive, simply thorough in a way that is beyond thorough, the way a scalpel is beyond a knife. She asks for clarifications. She cross-references internal sections. She notes three points where the documentation's language implies a certainty that the underlying data does not fully support, and she is correct about all three, and Pela — to her considerable credit — acknowledges all three without defensiveness and provides the underlying data on request. "The tunnel logs," Topaz says, at the two-hour mark, looking at Luka's documentation. Luka, who is at the table for exactly this, straightens almost imperceptibly. Seele, sitting two seats away, does not look at him. Her eyes are on Topaz. Her expression is the professional one — evaluative, careful, nothing given away. "The walkthrough records," Topaz says, "are remarkably detailed for what was essentially an informal surveillance operation." She looks at Luka. "You were documenting in real time." "Yes," Luka says. "Daily logs rather than weekly summaries." "Four times weekly. Sometimes more." "The documentation specifies twice weekly was the assigned frequency." "The tunnels were changing faster than twice weekly covered," Luka says, with the straightforwardness of someone who is not going to pretend the question is more complicated than it is. "So I went more." Topaz looks at him for a moment. Alongside her, Aventurine is looking at Luka with the not-looking-directly look, and Gepard watches him read Luka the same way he read the room when he arrived. "The reorientation event," Topaz says. "Corridor seven-alpha-four, day eleven. You documented it and forwarded it to Commander Gepard within—" She checks. "Forty minutes." "Yes." "What made you photograph the orientation shift." Luka looks at her. "It was different from the previous visit. Things that are different from the previous visit get documented." "That's the methodology." "That's the methodology," he agrees. Topaz makes a note. It is a specific kind of note — not the note of someone writing down information, but the note of someone recording an assessment of a person, which is different and which Luka clocks, Gepard can see it in the slight shift of his posture. Aventurine says, without looking up from the page he is reading: "How did you know the tunnels." Everyone looks at him. He appears to be reading a section of Pela's surface-level analysis and paying no attention to the tunnel logs conversation. Luka says: "What do you mean." "The walking logs." Aventurine turns a page. "On day three you stopped checking the map. The route notation stops referring to junction markers and starts referring to the corridors by the feel of them." He looks up, and the light eyes are doing the direct-looking now. "People navigate by feel when they know a place. You'd been in those tunnels twice." A pause. "How did you know them." The room is quiet. Luka looks at Aventurine. Gepard watches him decide what to say, and watches him land on the honest version, because — as Gepard has observed over the weeks of knowing him — Luka defaults to honest when he cannot locate a reason not to be. "I don't know," Luka says. Aventurine holds his gaze for a moment. The entertained expression is not present. Something else is — something more attentive, more considered. He nods, once, the nod of someone filing an answer, and looks back at the page. Topaz has been watching this exchange. She makes another note. This one, Gepard thinks, is longer than the previous one. —— In the evening, after the first day's session ends and the IPC visitors are settled in the palace's guest quarters, Gepard finds Seele in the administrative wing corridor — their corridor, the one with the south-facing windows — standing at the glass and looking at the city below. He has not seen her look like this before. She looks like she is listening to something. "Seele," he says. She turns. Her expression is the one she had at the nine-beta junction — not the hostile wariness, not the evaluation. The young, uncertain, very still one. "He asked how Luka knew the tunnels," she says. "Yes." "Aventurine." "Yes. He read it in the logs." He stops beside her at the window. "He's perceptive." "He's going to look into it." She says this with the certainty of someone who has been thinking about it since the moment the question was asked. "If he noticed that and thought it was worth asking—" "He noticed everything," Gepard says. "He's been noticing everything since he arrived. That's his job." She is quiet for a moment. "Do you think he knows something. About—" She stops. She looks at the city. "About why Luka knows those tunnels." He looks at her carefully. "Do you know why Luka knows those tunnels." "No," she says, and then, after a pause that has something in it: "Not completely." She presses her fingers briefly against the glass. "There are things I don't remember. I told you that." "You told Luka that. In the tunnel." She looks at him. Something in her expression registers the knowledge that he knows about the nine-beta conversation, and then lets it go — she has, he has observed, a very efficient relationship with information she cannot control. "Yes." A pause. "The junction where the branch goes left. The market district corridor." She looks at her hand against the glass. "There was something else today. When Aventurine asked the question." He waits. "A sound," she says. "In my head. When he asked how Luka knew the tunnels." She says this very carefully, the way you say something you are not sure you believe yet. "A specific sound. Like—" She stops. "Like something moving underground. Not threatening. Just — there. Present. And then it was gone." He is very still. "Seele," he says. "What did it sound like." She looks at him. Her eyes in the corridor light are very purple and very serious. "Like a second heartbeat," she says. "Somewhere below." —— That night, Luka cannot sleep. This is not unusual — he has not been sleeping well for three weeks, since the junction conversation and the branch going left and the market district he has never been to. But tonight is different. Tonight the not-sleeping has a specific content, not just the ambient not-settling of a body that knows something the mind hasn't caught yet. He is in his quarters in the Underworld, in the building adjacent to Svarog's workshop where he has lived for eight months, and it is past midnight, and he is looking at the ceiling. He is thinking about the question. How did you know the tunnels. He is thinking about the honest answer he gave, which is that he does not know. Which is true. Which has been true for six weeks and which he has been managing with the same approach he applies to everything he cannot immediately explain — document it, continue, wait for more information. He is thinking about the branch going left. He is thinking about a sound. He does not know where the sound comes from. It arrived an hour ago and has not left — not a sound he is hearing, not external, but internal, the way the Stellaron's frequency must be internal for Bronya, somewhere below conscious perception, present and rhythmic and not alarming, just there. A second heartbeat. He does not know why he thinks of it that way. He has not said this to anyone. He sits up. He sits on the edge of his bed in the dark of his Underworld quarters and he puts his hands on his knees and he breathes, and the sound is there, low and steady and familiar in the specific way that his body knows the route through those tunnels — not because he learned it, but because it is already in him, already known, the knowledge pre-existing the occasion for it. And with the sound comes something else. A fragment. Not a memory exactly — not enough context for a memory, not enough content. More like the edge of one, the way you see the edge of a photograph but not the image. Purple. The specific purple of a particular pair of eyes in a particular quality of light he cannot place, and a voice he does not recognize but which his body responds to with the specific quality it responds to the junction in nine-beta, with the recognition that arrives before the translation. The fragment lasts perhaps three seconds. Then it is gone. He sits on the edge of the bed in the dark for a long time afterward, his hands on his knees and the sound still present underneath everything, and he thinks about Svarog's words, and about the direction that matters, and about the branch going left in the market district he has never been to. He does not sleep for a long time. But when he does, it is deeper than it has been in weeks. As if something that has been waiting a very long time has found a crack in the surface and put the first thin root through, and the body, recognizing this, releases what it has been holding. —— The second day belongs to Aventurine. He requests, in the morning, to see the Stellaron's chamber. He asks for this before Topaz, before the documentation review continuation, before any of the other items on the formal itinerary, over the breakfast that the palace kitchen provides, in the tone of someone making a casual conversational request that is not casual at all. Bronya says: "Of course. This afternoon." He says: "This morning, if possible." She looks at him. He looks back with the light eyes and the entertained expression and the surface that is doing considerably less work than what is underneath it. She says: "This morning." They go at 0830. Gepard walks with them — with Bronya, Aventurine, and Topaz, through the palace's lower corridors and down the stairs to the construction chamber level, and then further down, through the passage that Bronya unlocked three weeks ago with the geomarrow key she keeps on her person. He has not been to the Stellaron's chamber before. He has been to the construction chamber dozens of times — has spent hours in it, has watched the array take shape in it, has stood in it with Bronya in the lamplight while she named the fear she had been holding for three months. He has been twenty-three meters above this room on many occasions without having been in it. It is different from what he expected, which is that he did not know what to expect and so finds that his body has supplied a default expectation in the form of the Fragmentum bloom sites — amber, crystalline, hostile. The chamber is none of those things. It is a large, low-ceilinged space carved from the palace's foundation rock, lit by a ring of geomarrow lanterns that emit a pale blue-gold light, and at its center, on a raised platform of the same foundation stone, sits a structure that is roughly the size of a large cabinet and which is completely unremarkable in every physical sense. The Stellaron. It does not look like an existential threat. It looks like a piece of stone with unusual light properties. He looks at it and thinks about Bronya hearing it for two months, hearing it climb, hearing the interval change, standing in the construction chamber above this room with the array humming and her hands open at her sides and the word quiet said in the register of someone remembering what quiet was. He looks at Aventurine. Aventurine is looking at the Stellaron with an expression he has not worn at any other point in the visit — stripped of the entertainment, stripped of the calculation. Something direct and unguarded, the expression of a person looking at something they have seen before in different circumstances and are remembering. Gepard files this. Topaz is beside Aventurine, and she is not looking at the Stellaron. She is looking at Aventurine looking at the Stellaron, and her expression has a quality of careful, private attention that is different from every professional expression she has worn in the past day — something warmer, something watchful, the way you watch someone you know well when they are in the presence of something that costs them something. Gepard files this too. "The array is above us," Bronya says, from beside him. Her voice is even. She is looking at the Stellaron the way she looked at it in the construction chamber — present, measured, the woman who has been listening to this thing for months and knows its frequencies personally. "Twenty-three meters. The disruption frequency propagates through the foundation geomarrow." She pauses. "The broadcast signal has been at 1.3 for three weeks." "I know," Aventurine says. "I've been reviewing the readings." A pause. "The readings we transmitted," Bronya says, "or your own." Aventurine looks at her. The entertainment is back, but changed — less a surface and more an acknowledgment. "Both," he says. Bronya looks at him for a moment. She looks at Topaz. She makes a decision that Gepard can read in the precise way her posture shifts — not defensive, not aggressive, the decision to press. "You were monitoring Belobog before the letter," she says. "The broadcast escalation. The bloom events." She looks at Aventurine. "You have your own readings going back further than you've indicated." "We have readings going back some time," Topaz says, which is the careful version of yes. "How long," Bronya says. A pause. "Long enough," Aventurine says, which is the same answer Bronya gave him yesterday, reflected back, and which is a message and they both know it. She looks at him. He looks at her. The Stellaron sits between them on its platform, pale blue-gold in the geomarrow light, at 1.3 and holding, and Gepard stands beside Bronya and watches Aventurine decide how much to give and decides he does not like any of the possible answers. "You've known about the Stellaron under Belobog for longer than the Everwinter Hill event," Bronya says. Not a question. "The IPC has extensive monitoring infrastructure," Topaz says. "I know what the IPC has," Bronya says. "I'm asking how long you've known about this one." The chamber is quiet. The geomarrow lanterns hum. Aventurine is looking at Bronya with the expression he had when she walked into the entrance hall — recalibration, the assessment updating — and when he speaks, it is without the entertainment, without the surface, just the stripped version, which Gepard suspects is something he does not often allow. "Long enough," he says again, and this time it does not mean the same as it did a moment ago. This time it means: longer than you want to know, and I am not going to tell you in this room, and there is a reason for that, and the reason is not what you're afraid it is. Bronya holds his gaze for a long moment. Then she turns back to the Stellaron, and says: "There's something else you should know." Gepard looks at her. She looks at the Stellaron. She has the expression she gets when she has made a decision about information — not withholding it, not managing it, simply choosing the moment. She has been choosing this moment, he realizes, since the letter arrived. She has been waiting for this room, this conversation, this specific exchange that has confirmed what she suspected about how much the IPC already knows. She reaches into her coat and takes out a document. She places it on the surface beside the Stellaron's platform. "Three weeks ago," she says, "I began receiving a secondary signal." The chamber is very quiet. "Not from this Stellaron," she says. "A second one. Deeper." She looks at Aventurine. "You already know there is a second Stellaron beneath Belobog." It is not a question. Aventurine does not deny it. And that is the answer. —— Notes: Aventurine read Luka's logs and asked the one question nobody else thought to ask, in the tone of someone who already had the beginning of an answer. 'How did you know the tunnels.' I have been waiting to write that question for three chapters. Topaz watching Aventurine in the Stellaron's chamber — the warm, careful, private attention. She watches him the way you watch someone you know well when they are in the presence of something that costs them something. There is a history there. We will find out what it is. Bronya waited for that room, that conversation, to say what she knew. She let Aventurine confirm how much the IPC already knew before she gave him the second Stellaron. That is not a concession. That is strategy. She gave him something after he gave her something, and now they are in an exchange rather than an interrogation, and that is entirely on purpose. The second Stellaron is in the room now. It has been named. The IPC knows about it. Bronya knows about it. Gepard is learning it in real time and I will not be held responsible for how that lands. Luka's fragment: purple eyes in unknown light. A voice his body knows before his mind does. He slept deeply afterward, for the first time in weeks. The first root through the crack. There will be more. Seele heard the second heartbeat. She described it as something below. She does not know what it means yet. She will.
