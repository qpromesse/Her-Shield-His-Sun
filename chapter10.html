Chapter 10: "What You Don't Remember"

Notes: (See the end of the chapter for notes.) Chapter Text There is a moment, three days after the text at 2340, where Gepard walks into the office and she is standing at the window. Not at the desk, not with a folder, not in motion between one task and another — just standing at the window, looking out at the upper city, with her arms loose at her sides and her hair down, which she does not usually wear down during working hours and which she has apparently forgotten about. The morning light comes through the glass and sits on the side of her face, and she has the expression that only exists when she does not know anyone is watching — unmanaged, unheld, completely her own. He stops in the doorway. He knows, with the certainty of someone who has been paying attention for five years, that he should announce himself. A knock, a cleared throat, something to give her the half-second she needs to reassemble the architecture that the window and the quiet morning have temporarily taken down. He does not do this. He stands in the doorway for three seconds and looks at her the way he does not let himself look when she can see him — fully, without the careful peripheral management, without the practiced discipline of looking slightly to the left of the thing he cannot look at directly. Three seconds of looking at the line of her standing in the morning light, at the loose hair and the open hands and the face that is just her face, and then he does the correct thing, which is knock on the open door frame. She turns. She sees him. Something moves through her expression — not the surprise of being caught, exactly, but the specific quality of someone recalibrating from a private state to a shared one, and finding the distance between those two states to be shorter than it used to be. She does not fully reassemble. She looks at him for a moment with the window light still on her face and the hair still down, and she says: "You're early." "The morning brief was moved up. I sent word—" "I saw it." She moves toward the desk, reaching for the clasp on the table to pin her hair back. "I was just—" She stops. The clasp is in her hand. She looks at it. She sets it down. "Give me the perimeter update." He gives her the perimeter update. He stands in the doorway the whole time and does not step fully into the room, because he is aware, with a clarity that is becoming difficult to ignore, that if he closes the distance between the doorway and the desk he is going to be standing approximately where he was standing in the stairwell three weeks ago, and the stairwell still lives in him the way that certain mornings do — specific, present, not settling. She listens to the perimeter update with the hair clasp still on the desk and her hair still down, and at the end of it she looks at him across the room with the expression she has been wearing for thirteen days, the less-sealed one, and she says: "Come in, Gepard. You don't have to stand in the door." He comes in. He closes the distance. He sits in the chair from the corner, which is at the desk, which means he is sitting close enough that if he set his patrol report down and she set her briefing folder down and neither of them moved back, they would be near enough for the quality of near to become a different question entirely. He sets the patrol report on the desk. She does not move back. "The administrator briefing is at ten," she says. "Yes." "I want you there." "I'll be there." She looks at him. He looks at her. The clasp is between them on the desk, and the hair is still down, and the morning light is doing whatever it does, and neither of them picks up their respective documents for a moment that is approximately four seconds long and feels considerably longer. Then she picks up the briefing folder. He picks up the patrol report. And the four seconds are over, and they work, and the thing that was present in those four seconds does not go away — it settles, the way a thing settles when it has been acknowledged without being named, becoming part of the texture of the room. At some point, without planning to, he reaches across the desk to move a map that is covering the eastern perimeter notation she needs, and his hand passes close to hers — not touching, but close, the specific proximity where you are aware of the warmth of another person's skin without contact — and she does not move her hand away. She finishes writing the notation. He moves the map. Their hands are near each other for perhaps two seconds in the vicinity of the desk, and then they are not, and neither of them says anything about it, and the thing in the room gets slightly heavier. —— The administrator briefing goes well, which Gepard measures by the fact that no one leaves the room in open panic and Administrator Venn, who has been Bronya's most persistent institutional obstacle for three weeks, asks two intelligent follow-up questions about the Stellaron containment protocol and accepts her answers with the look of a man who is updating his priors in real time. After it, they walk back through the palace together — the route they have been taking together for two weeks, through the administrative wing's outer passage with the north-facing windows. The same route she walked with Seele. The same route he walked with Seele. The corridor that has become, without formal designation, their corridor, the one that belongs to the category of things that have acquired meaning through repetition without anyone deciding they should. She says, midway through it: "Venn asked good questions." "He did." "He's going to be useful. Once he finishes deciding whether to trust me." "He's almost there." She glances at him. "You think so." "The second question. He wouldn't have asked it if he wasn't leaning toward you. He was checking whether your answer would match what he already suspects is true." He pauses. "It did." She is quiet for a step. Two. "You were watching him." "I watch everyone in those rooms." "I know you do." Another pause. The light through the windows makes moving shapes on the corridor floor as they walk, the pale winter sun doing its limited best. "What else did you see." "Pela is going to need a research assistant. She's been working at a pace that isn't sustainable and she won't say so." He glances at her. "She'll accept it if you frame it as resourcing rather than concern." Bronya looks at him. "I'll talk to her today." "And Seele — she's been taking on more than was assigned to her. The civilian alert coordination expanded into resettlement logistics at some point in the last week and no one formally authorized the expansion, she just absorbed it." "Because it needed doing." "Yes. But she needs the formal authority or she's going to hit administrative walls." He pauses. "Give her the title. She's already doing the job." She is looking at him as they walk. He is aware of this — the specific quality of her attention when it is fully on him, which has a weight to it that he has spent years learning to receive without showing that he receives it, with declining success. "You notice everything," she says. "It's my job." "No," she says, quietly. "Parts of it are your job. The rest is—" She stops. They have reached the stairwell landing, the one with the window that looks south, the one where she stood three weeks ago and told him the accurate version. She stops at the top of the landing and turns to look at him, and he stops too, because she has stopped, and because he was not going to walk past her when she stopped, and because this is simply the thing his body does in response to hers, which is another fact he has filed and not looked at. "The rest is you," she says. He looks at her. She is standing at the landing window with the pale light behind her, her hair still down from this morning, the briefing folder in her hand, and she is looking at him the way she has been looking at him with increasing frequency and decreasing management since the array — directly, with the full weight of it, not deflecting to the desk or the map or the folder. She is looking at him and she looks like she has made a decision, or is about to, or is standing at the edge of one. "Bronya," he says, and his voice comes out lower than he intends and he does not correct it. "I know," she says. "I know what I'm doing." "I'm not sure you do," he says, which is not what he means, and she knows it's not what he means, because what he means is I'm not sure I do, and they both know that, and the words are just the professional architecture making one last structural argument before it stops being able to hold the weight. She takes one step toward him. Not a decisive step, not a closing-of-distance step — a single, measured step, the kind that shortens the space between them by enough to change the quality of it, so that he can see the specific grey of her eyes clearly and the morning light catches the edge of her hair and the warmth of her proximity is a fact rather than an approximation. She tilts her head back slightly to look at him, because she is looking up and he is looking down, and this has always been the geometry of standing close to her — the specific angle of it, the way it makes the space between them feel more deliberate than it would be between two people of the same height, because both of them have to choose it. They are both choosing it. "I have the council session at two," she says, which is not what she means either, and they both know it. "I know," he says. "And the east wall final certification is tomorrow morning." "I know." "And Serval is leaving on Thursday." A pause. "She keeps telling me she'll come back." Another pause. "I believe her." "She will," he says. "She always does." They are standing close enough that his exhale moves a strand of her hair, and she does not step back, and he does not step back, and the stairwell landing is very quiet in the way that spaces are quiet when the world has arranged itself around a single point of attention. "Gepard," she says, and his name in her voice at this range, at this volume, in this register, does something to him that he has no adequate description for and does not attempt one. "Yes," he says. She looks at him. She looks at him the way she looked at the frequency guide when the array fractured the feedback loop — like something that has been a sound in her head for a very long time is finally taking a form she can see clearly. And then her hand — the left one, the one that trembled in the array room, the one that she stilled deliberately and he looked away from — comes up, very slowly, and rests against his jaw. She is not reaching up to him the way she reaches for armor pieces or documents or the things on high shelves that require effort. She is simply — placing her hand there. Intentionally. The cold of her palm against the side of his face, the tips of her fingers just past his jaw, and she is looking at him the whole time with those grey eyes that are very close and very serious and entirely unmanaged. He does not breathe. He does not move. He stands on the stairwell landing with her hand on his face and the morning light behind her and five years of not-moving catching up to him all at once, and he brings his hand up — slowly, the specific slowness of someone being deliberate, not tentative — and covers hers. Not moving it. Not pulling it away. Just his hand over hers, holding it there, the warmth of both of them together against the side of his face. She exhales. It is a small sound. The same quality as the array room, when the pressure set down — not dramatic, just honest, just the body acknowledging something real. "You're going to be late to the council session," he says, after a moment, because one of them has to say something and because it is true and because he cannot, in good conscience, be responsible for what he might say instead. "I'm always early to the council session," she says, and does not move her hand. He looks at her. She looks at him. His hand is over hers and her palm is still against his jaw and the stairwell landing has, without any formal announcement, become somewhere completely different from where it was three minutes ago. "Bronya," he says. "Don't," she says, gently. "Don't say we should be careful or that this is complicated or anything—" She stops. Her thumb moves, slightly, against his cheek — an involuntary thing, he thinks, and then thinks she probably didn't mean to do it, and then thinks it doesn't matter, and then stops thinking. "I know it's complicated," she says. "I've known it's complicated for—" She pauses. "A while." "Me too," he says. "I know," she says, and the corner of her mouth moves — the genuine one, the one that is not the almost-smile but is the thing the almost-smile was always pointing toward. "You're not subtle." He looks at her. "You're excellent at many things," she says, and there is something in her voice that is warm and precise and slightly terrible because it is her voice in that register at this range. "Subtle is not among them." "I thought I was managing it," he says. "You were managing it," she says. "I was aware of everything you were managing." A pause. "Since approximately—" She stops. Her eyes do the thing they do when she is calculating, the faint unfocus of someone retrieving a specific memory. "Since the winter assessment in our second year. When you stood outside the briefing room for six minutes before coming in." He stares at her. "I counted," she says. He stares at her. "You were seventeen," she says, and the warmth in her voice has something under it that has been there for considerably longer than three weeks or twelve days or any of the intervals he has been measuring. "And you knocked on an open door, which I had never seen anyone do before, and you stood at attention the entire briefing even though I told you to sit down, and afterwards you told me one thing that was wrong with my analysis and two things that were right and I—" She stops. She looks at him directly. "I noticed," she says. "I have been noticing. For a long time." He holds her gaze. "Why didn't you say anything," he says. "You know why," she says. And he does. He knows exactly why — the title, the rank, the space between them that was constructed carefully and for good reason and which they have been, gradually and then all at once, dismantling. "And I was afraid," she says, which costs her something, and she says it anyway. "That I was wrong. About what I was seeing." "You weren't wrong," he says. "I know that now," she says. His thumb moves across the back of her hand, the same involuntary echo of what she did a moment ago, and she feels it and looks down at their joined hands against his jaw and then back up at him, and the morning light is doing what it does and the stairwell is quiet and neither of them moves for a moment that stretches itself out between them like something that does not want to end. "The council session," he says eventually, very quietly. "In ninety minutes," she says. "Seele is going to wonder where you are." "Seele," she says, with the specific quality she uses when she is thinking about what Seele knows and has chosen not to say, "is going to have opinions about this regardless of when I show up." He breathes. He looks at her. He makes a decision that is perhaps the most deliberate one he has made in five years, which is to turn his head very slightly and press his lips, briefly, to her palm. It is not a large gesture. It does not need to be. The geometry of the stairwell and the five years and the morning light have already done the work — this is just the confirmation of it, quiet and contained and entirely without the drama it would probably warrant if either of them were built for drama. She goes still. Not the bracing stillness. Not the managed stillness. The stillness of someone receiving something they have been waiting for for a long time and are choosing to hold completely, without moving, so as not to lose any part of it. He lifts his head. She is looking at him with no composure requirement anywhere on her face. "Ninety minutes," he says. "Ninety minutes," she agrees. She takes her hand back, slowly, and the warmth of her palm leaves his jaw, and he lets her hand go and she takes it back and they are standing on the stairwell landing again, which is the same place they have been standing, and is entirely different. She straightens. She picks up her briefing folder. She looks at him once more with that face — the face without the armor, the one that has been appearing more and more frequently and which he is going to have to figure out how to look at directly or lose his mind entirely. "Don't be late to the council session," she says. "I'm never late," he says. "I know," she says, and goes. He stands on the landing for a moment. He breathes once. Twice. He does not go anywhere for thirty seconds. Then he picks up his patrol folder, and goes down the stairs, and is not late to the council session, and manages — with considerable effort — to read every line of the agenda. —— In corridor nine-beta, on the same morning, Luka is on his fourth walkthrough of the week when he finds Seele there again. This time she is not carrying a supply crate. She is crouched at the base of the wall where the crystallization is thickest, her hand hovering close to the amber-black surface, not touching, the specific posture of someone who has come here to look at something rather than pass through. She does not hear him coming. He stops several meters back, because the quality of her attention is the kind that should not be interrupted, and he watches her crouch in the tunnel's amber light and look at the wall with an expression he cannot quite read from this distance. He makes a sound with his boot on the ground — deliberate, a small warning — and she turns. She sees him. She stands. They look at each other. "You're here early," he says. "I could say the same." "Fourth walkthrough this week," he says, and does not know why he tells her this. She looks at him. Her eyes are very purple in the tunnel lighting, a specific shade he has not seen except on her, and there is something in her expression that is the same as the last time — the hostile wariness, the evaluation — but there is something underneath it that was not there the last time, or that he did not notice, which is a version of noticing that feels like a hand on the back of his neck. "Why do you come four times," she says. "The job is twice a week." "I know what Sampo told you. I'm asking why you come four times." He considers this. "The tunnels are different every time. Even when nothing changes, they're different." He pauses. "I want to know what they look like when they're different." She looks at him for a moment with an expression he cannot classify. Then she looks back at the wall. "The crystallization here," she says. "It's dulling faster in this section than the others." "I know. I've been logging it." He takes out his sensor by habit and looks at the reading. Yellow. Same as the others. He shows her the screen without deciding to. "Has been for four days." She looks at the screen. She looks at the wall. She does the thing she does — the gathering of words, the arrangement of them — and then she says, without preamble: "Do you ever feel like you know a place you've never been." He looks at her. "These tunnels," she says, still looking at the wall. "I've been in the Underworld my whole life, mostly. And I've been in these corridors twice. But they feel—" She stops. "Familiar." He says nothing. She turns and looks at him with the evaluation back in her eyes, the wariness — but the wariness has a different quality now, the quality of someone who is not wary of a stranger but of something they cannot explain, which is a more unsettling variety of wary. "That's strange," she says. "Isn't it." "Yes," he says. "Does it happen to you." He looks at the tunnel. He looks at the route he has walked from memory four times without checking the map once. He looks at her face and the specific purple of her eyes and the particular way she holds herself in a space, with that coiled readiness, the weight balanced forward on the balls of her feet, the way someone holds themselves when they grew up in an environment that required fast reactions. Something about this posture. Something about this posture is— He does not finish the thought, because it does not have a finish that makes sense. It is more of a shape than a thought — the shape of something half-recognized, like a word in a language you studied once and have mostly forgotten, appearing in a context where you do not expect it and the meaning arrives before the translation does. "Sometimes," he says. She looks at him. He looks at her. The tunnel is quiet. The dulling amber wall is at their backs. The sensor in his hand reads yellow. She says, carefully, like she is testing the weight of it: "I don't know why I keep coming back here." "The supply checks—" "I've done those. I come after the supply checks." She looks at him directly. "I don't know why." He holds her gaze. He does not look away. She does not look away. They stand in corridor nine-beta with the settled crystallization and the quiet and the amber light and the thing between them that has no name because it has no origin they can access, and the recognition that is not recognition because they cannot remember what there is to recognize. "I don't know why I come four times either," he says, honestly. Something shifts in her expression. The wariness does not leave — he suspects it never fully leaves, that it is too structural to her to simply lift — but something around it softens, slightly, in the specific way that happens when two people discover they share something they did not expect to share. "Your readings are accurate," she says, which is, from Seele, approximately equivalent to a formal declaration of truce. "The rate of dulling. Your logs are accurate." "Thank you," he says. "Don't make it a thing," she says, which is exactly what Pela said to Gepard ten days ago in a different corridor of the same building, and which he does not know, and which means nothing except that Belobog apparently produces a specific variety of person who does things because they are right and does not want gratitude for them. She moves to leave. She does not rush it this time — less of the fast purposeful exit, more of the ordinary movement of someone concluding a conversation. At the junction she stops, and he expects the one-more-thing, and she does it, but it is not what he expects. "Luka," she says. He waits. "Have you ever—" She stops. She is looking at the junction wall, the place where seven-alpha and nine-beta branch away from each other, and her expression has the quality of someone trying to say something they do not have the right words for. "Have you ever looked at someone and felt like you already know how they're going to finish their sentences." A pause. "Before they finish them." He looks at her profile in the tunnel light. "Sometimes," he says, which is the same word he used before, and they both know it. She nods. Slowly. She does not explain further and he does not ask her to. She takes the nine-beta branch and her footsteps go quiet around the bend, and he stands at the junction and looks at the wall and breathes the cold tunnel air and tries to find the end of the thought that has no end, the half-recognition that arrived before the translation did.
